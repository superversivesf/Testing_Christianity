# Testing Christianity — Summaries & Fact-Check

Source: YouTube playlist `PL5Ea1RHP4JqpZlrJxYOcWeU54xSeo_BJi` ("Truth Unedited" channel), 15 parts, ~8.5 hours. Transcripts (.txt and .srt) are in `transcripts/`; original `.mp4` files are kept alongside.

## Framing note

The series argues from a sacred-name / Hebrew-Israelite frame ("Yahuwah" for God, "Yahushua" for Jesus). The thesis: mainstream Christianity is a "religion of tares" — a Roman/Greek hybrid that hijacked a Hebrew covenant — and believers must abandon the label "Christian," reject Sunday worship, the Greek name "Jesus," and replacement theology, and identify with scattered Israel (which the speaker identifies with the African diaspora — see the related "We Are Not Black People" series referenced in Part 5).

**What I'll fact-check below**: dated historical claims, document attributions, etymologies, and claims about scholarly consensus — things with verifiable answers.

**What I won't adjudicate**: theological/interpretive claims (covenant theology, sacred-name doctrine, Sabbath day, Trinity, Pauline authority, the speaker's identity claims about biblical Israel). Reasonable scholars and believers disagree on these; calling them "false" would be a category error.

## Per-episode summaries

### Part 1 — Introduction: The Test (24 min)
Frames the series. Argues Christianity is the world's largest religion (~31% of population, 2.5 billion) and asks whether that scale is compatible with Jesus' "narrow gate" saying. Recounts the speaker's personal journey leaving Baptist/non-denominational Christianity. Ends by issuing a 5-question "test" to give to other Christians: (1) who is the New Covenant with? (2) what scripture gives the covenant for Christians? (3) what does the Father plan to do in the last days? (4) what are you waiting for him to do with Christians? (5) heaven or earth?

### Part 2 — But the word "Christian" is in my Bible (24 min)
Tackles the obvious objection that Acts 11:26 and 1 Peter 4:16 use "Christian." Argues the word entered through Greek translation as a Hellenizing imposition; the apostles called themselves followers of "the Way." Claims Peter spoke Hebrew and needed Mark as his Greek interpreter, citing Irenaeus and Clement of Alexandria. Quotes Josephus saying Jews didn't encourage learning foreign languages.

### Part 3 — The religion of Christianity is the tares Satan planted (33 min)
Lays out the core thesis using the Parable of the Wheat and Tares (Matt 13:24–43). Christianity, the speaker argues, is the counterfeit-but-indistinguishable "tare" planted alongside the apostles' true seed; only at the harvest does the difference show. Flags "replacement theology" (church replaces Israel) as the central error. The speaker identifies the African diaspora as scattered Israel.

### Part 4 — Take the test! (40 min)
Walks through the five test questions and the answers people gave in comments. Concludes that the New Covenant is exclusively with "the house of Israel and the house of Judah" (Jer 31:31–34 / Heb 8:8–11), that there is *no* covenant scripture for "Christians," that the Father's last-days plan is gathering scattered Israel from the four corners (Jer 23:3–8; Isa 43:1–6), and that the eternal kingdom is on earth, not heaven.

### Part 5 — The enemy has a plan for Christians (26 min)
Shifts from doctrine to apocalyptic motivation. Cites the "Albert Pike letter to Mazzini" (1871) as evidence of a Masonic three-world-wars plan culminating in disillusioned Christians being herded toward "the doctrine of Lucifer." Connects this to current Israel/Islam tensions. **(See fact-check #1 below.)**

### Part 6 — What happened after the book of Acts (25 min)
Historical recap: Paul before Nero c. 62 CE, the Jewish War (66–73), Vespasian, Titus, the Temple destroyed in 70 CE, Masada in 73, the Bar Kokhba revolt 132–136, Hadrian renaming Judaea to Syria-Palestina and Jerusalem to Aelia Capitolina. Frames it as the gap between the apostles and the institutional church.

### Part 7 — The unspoken history about the birth of Christianity (35 min)
Argues Roman Gentile believers received Jesus through pre-existing Greek philosophical categories rather than Hebrew prophecy. Introduces the cult of Serapis (Greco-Egyptian deity of the Ptolemaic period), claims pagans worshipping Serapis were already called "Christianos" before Jesus, and cites the alleged Hadrian letter to Servianus from the *Historia Augusta*. **(See fact-check #2 and #3.)**

### Part 8 — The early church fathers created Christianity (35 min)
Profiles Eusebius, Irenaeus, Justin Martyr, Lactantius, Origen, and Tertullian, then quotes (from David Bercot's *Dictionary of Early Christian Beliefs*) their statements that read as supersessionist toward Jews — e.g., Justin Martyr: "we are the true spiritual Israel." Concludes these men, not the apostles, founded "Christianity," and this is the doctrinal root the speaker rejects.

### Part 9 — How the New Testament became centered around Paul (35 min)
Discusses Eusebius' canon list and the role of Marcion (mid-2nd century) — who accepted only Luke and Paul's letters and rejected the Old Testament — in forcing the early church to define a canon, with the unintended effect of elevating Paul. Discusses the Ebionites (Jewish Jesus-followers who kept Torah and rejected Paul). The speaker doesn't reject Paul but says Paul is widely *misused* (citing 2 Pet 3:15–16).

### Part 10 — The moment the world became Christian (28 min)
Constantine: vision, Edict of Milan (313), Council of Nicaea (325), suppression of the Ebionites/Quartodecimans, Sunday observance, anti-Jewish measures at Nicaea. Introduces "syncretism" — the deliberate blending of pagan and biblical elements — as the mechanism. Claims Constantine made Christianity the "official religion of the Roman Empire" in 324–325. **(See fact-check #4.)**

### Part 11 — The road to the Trinity: you must understand Greek philosophy (25 min)
Sets up the Trinity discussion (which actually arrives later) by introducing Greek philosophy. Goes back to Thales of Miletus (c. 6th c. BCE) as the first Greek philosopher and traces the shift from mythology to deductive reasoning. Argues the New World — including U.S. democracy, education, and Christianity itself — is "Hellenized."

### Part 12 — The Greek philosophers: prophets of the Greeks (45 min — longest episode)
Walks through Pythagoras, Socrates, Plato, Aristotle. Argues Plato's *Republic* contains a prophecy of "the just man" who will be "crucified" (Book 2, 361e–362a) and that early Christians treated Plato as the Greeks' Isaiah. Claims Origen formulated the Trinity by adapting Plato's "one and many" via Neo-Platonism. **(See fact-check #5 and #6.)**

### Part 13 — Alexander the Great and Hellenization (30 min)
History of Alexander, the diadochi (Seleucids, Ptolemies), and the spreading of Greek language and culture. Defines "Hellenization" as Satan's strategy to undo what God did at Babel by re-uniting humanity under one culture. Claims modern Christianity continues Hellenization. Touches on the formation of the Septuagint. **(See fact-check #7.)**

### Part 14 — The Maccabean Revolt (35 min)
Antiochus IV Epiphanes' decrees outlawing Torah, the Tobiads, the desecration of the Temple, Mattathias and Judah Maccabee, the rededication of the Temple on 25 Kislev 165 BCE (Hanukkah). Used to argue that the Yahudim of Judea actively *rejected* Hellenization, so any modern claim that Jews casually adopted Greek culture misses the point.

### Part 15 — The 8th day: why people go to church on Sunday (39 min)
The most evidence-dense episode. Quotes Justin Martyr's *First Apology* ch. 67 and *Dialogue with Trypho* (chs. 10, 11, 12, 24, 41) showing 2nd-century Christian rejection of Sabbath and circumcision and the "eighth day" symbolism for Sunday. Quotes Clement of Alexandria's claim that Plato prophesied the Lord's day (*Stromata* book 5) and the Roman Catholic Catechism §§2174–2175 calling Sunday the "fulfillment of the Sabbath." Concludes Sunday observance is patristic doctrine, not apostolic.

## Fact-checks

### 1. The "Albert Pike to Mazzini" three-world-wars letter (Part 5) — **Not authentic**

**Claim**: An 1871 letter from Albert Pike to Giuseppe Mazzini detailing a Masonic plan for three world wars was on display in the British Museum Library until 1977; the British Museum now denies it ever existed.

**Finding**: There is no archival evidence such a letter ever existed in the British Museum or British Library. Multiple Freedom-of-Information requests to the British Library (the manuscripts portion of the British Museum was transferred to the British Library in 1973) returned the same answer: no record, no catalog entry, no exhibit. The "letter" in circulation traces to William Guy Carr's 1958 book *Pawns in the Game*, which did not cite a source. The "Lucifer is the god of Freemasonry" attribution often paired with Pike's *Morals and Dogma* originates from Léo Taxil's 1894–1897 hoax, which Taxil publicly admitted to fabricating.

**Sources**:
- [British Library FOI request: "Pike 3 World Wars"](https://www.whatdotheyknow.com/request/pike_3_world_wars) — Library response: not in catalog
- [Fact-check: Albert Pike Three World Wars Letter](https://factually.co/fact-checks/history/albert-pike-three-world-wars-letter-primary-source-775c6a)
- [British Library FOI: Confirmation of the Albert Pike letter](https://www.whatdotheyknow.com/request/confirmation_of_the_albert_pike)

The video presents the alleged letter's "predictions" of Israel/Islam conflict as evidence the plan is unfolding, but the text dates to the mid-20th century at the earliest and was written *after* Israel existed.

### 2. Hadrian's letter to Servianus saying Christians worship Serapis (Part 7) — **Almost certainly a 4th-century forgery**

**Claim**: A 134 CE letter from Emperor Hadrian to his brother-in-law Servianus says: "those who worship Serapis are, in fact, Christians, and those who call themselves bishops of Christ are, in fact, devotees of Serapis." The video cites *Historia Augusta* vol. 3.

**Finding**: The letter is in the *Historia Augusta*, but modern classical scholarship considers it a forgery. The internal chronology is impossible — Hadrian was in Egypt in 130 CE, Servianus was consul in 134 CE, and Hadrian adopted Lucius Aelius Caesar (referenced in the letter) in 136 CE — meaning the letter could not have been written during any single moment that all three conditions held. The reference to a "Patriarch" of the Jewish community fits the late 4th century, not Hadrian's reign (the office didn't exist before the suppression of the Bar Kokhba revolt in 135). The *Historia Augusta* itself is now generally regarded as the work of a single author writing under multiple pseudonyms in the late 4th century.

**Sources**:
- [Historia Augusta — Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Historia_Augusta) — overview of authorship problems
- [Historia Augusta on UChicago/Penelope (Loeb)](https://penelope.uchicago.edu/Thayer/E/Roman/Texts/Historia_Augusta/Hadrian/1*.html) — primary text

The video's broader claim that pagans were called "Christianos" before Jesus on account of Serapis worship has no support outside this disputed letter; the standard etymology has the term originating in Antioch in the 40s CE (Acts 11:26, supported by Tacitus *Annals* 15.44).

### 3. "The Antiquities of the Jews was originally written in Hebrew" (Part 6) — **Misattribution**

**Claim**: Josephus' *Antiquities of the Jews* was first written in Hebrew, then translated to Greek.

**Finding**: This is the standard claim about Josephus' *earlier* work, the *Jewish War* — Josephus himself says (BJ 1.3) he first wrote it "in our native tongue" (most scholars: Aramaic, possibly with a Hebrew/Aramaic original) for diaspora Jews and Parthians, then rendered it into Greek. *Antiquities* (completed c. 93/94 CE) was composed directly in Greek for a Greco-Roman audience. The video appears to conflate the two works.

**Source**: [Britannica — Flavius Josephus](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Flavius-Josephus)

### 4. "Constantine made Christianity the official religion of the Roman Empire in 324–325" (Part 10) — **Off by ~55 years**

**Claim**: Constantine, after defeating Licinius, "officially declared himself a Christian and made Christianity the official religion of the Roman Empire."

**Finding**: Constantine *legalized* Christianity (Edict of Milan, 313 CE) and patronized it heavily, but did not establish it as the state religion. Christianity became the official state religion under Theodosius I via the **Edict of Thessalonica** issued **27 February 380 CE** — 43 years after Constantine's death. The edict was issued jointly by Theodosius I, Gratian, and Valentinian II and required all subjects to profess the Nicene faith. As the History Today summary puts it, Constantine was content to legalize and endow; Theodosius "put the brute force of the imperial state behind the faith."

**Sources**:
- [Edict of Thessalonica — Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Edict_of_Thessalonica)
- [Christianity as the Roman state religion — Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christianity_as_the_Roman_state_religion)
- [The Edict of Thessalonica — History Today](https://www.historytoday.com/archive/months-past/edict-thessalonica)

### 5. "Plato prophesied the crucified Christ in *Republic* Book 2" (Part 12) — **Genre misreading**

**Claim**: Plato's *Republic* 361e–362a, where the perfectly just man "will be crucified," is a Greek "prophecy" of the Christian Messiah, on which the early church fathers built their faith.

**Finding**: The passage is real. Glaucon (not Socrates, and not Plato in his own voice) is constructing a thought experiment: take a perfectly *just* man with a reputation for *injustice* versus a perfectly *unjust* man with a reputation for *justice*, and ask which life is happier. The just man being "crucified" (the Greek term ἀνασχινδυλευθήσεται more literally means "impaled on a stake") is a worst-case scenario meant to strip rewards from justice so the question of whether justice is *intrinsically* worth pursuing can be examined cleanly. Glaucon is steel-manning the *opponent's* position so Socrates has to refute it.

It is **not** a forecast of a coming savior. Some early Christians (notably Justin Martyr and Clement of Alexandria) drew typological parallels, but those are interpretations *applied to* Plato, not prophecies *issued by* Plato.

**Sources**:
- [Plato, *Republic* 2.362a — Perseus](http://www.perseus.tufts.edu/hopper/text?doc=Perseus:text:1999.01.0168:book%3D2:section%3D362a) — primary text
- [Plato the Prophet? — *Antigone Journal*](https://antigonejournal.com/2024/03/plato-prophet-crucifixion/) — scholarly discussion of how the passage was later read messianically and why that reading goes beyond what Plato wrote

### 6. "Origen founded/created the doctrine of the Trinity" (Parts 8, 11, 12) — **Oversimplifies a multi-century development**

**Claim**: Origen (c. 185–253 CE) is the "founder of the Trinity" via Neo-Platonism's "one and many."

**Finding**: The Trinity doctrine has multiple contributors and was not created by any single father:

- **Tertullian** (c. 160–225 CE) coined the *Latin* term *trinitas* and the formula *una substantia, tres personae* — he is the proximate source of the Western vocabulary.
- **Origen** contributed important christological and pneumatological building blocks (eternal generation of the Son) that the later Cappadocian Fathers drew on, but he was *condemned posthumously* (553 CE, Second Council of Constantinople) for views including subordinationism, which is the opposite of orthodox Trinitarianism. Modern scholars debate how much his actual position was subordinationist vs. how much it was caricatured by his opponents.
- **Athanasius** (c. 296–373) and the **Cappadocian Fathers** (Basil of Caesarea, Gregory of Nyssa, Gregory of Nazianzus) did the heaviest lifting, formalized at the Councils of Nicaea (325) and Constantinople (381).

So calling Origen "the founder" overstates his role and elides Tertullian and the Cappadocians.

**Sources**:
- [Tertullian — Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tertullian)
- [Cappadocian Fathers — Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cappadocian_Fathers)
- [Origen — Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Origen)

### 7. "Pentateuch means seventy" / "70 scribes translated the Pentateuch and that's why it's called the Septuagint" (Part 13) — **Two terms conflated**

**Claim**: "[The translation] is called the Pentateuch which means the 70 and because 70 learned scribes were a part of this translation the Pentateuch was called the Septuagint."

**Finding**: Two different Greek words, two different meanings:

- **Pentateuch** = Greek *pentáteukhos*, "five scrolls/books" — the first five books of the Bible (Genesis through Deuteronomy). It has nothing to do with the number seventy.
- **Septuagint** (LXX) = from Latin *septuaginta*, "seventy" — the Greek translation of the Hebrew scriptures. The legend in the *Letter of Aristeas* says **72** translators (six from each of the twelve tribes) produced the translation under Ptolemy II Philadelphus (3rd c. BCE); the name "Septuagint/seventy" rounds 72 down. The legend originally covered only the Pentateuch and was later extended to the entire Greek Old Testament.

**Sources**:
- [Septuagint — Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Septuagint)
- [Letter of Aristeas — Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Letter_of_Aristeas)

### 8. "Peter cut off the ear of a Roman soldier" (Part 2) — **Wrong group**

**Claim**: Peter "cut off the ear of a Roman soldier."

**Finding**: John 18:10 says Peter cut off the ear of *Malchus, the high priest's servant*. The arresting party at Gethsemane was led by temple police acting for the chief priests, not Roman legionaries (a Roman cohort is mentioned in John 18:3 but Malchus is named explicitly as the high priest's servant). Minor, but factually checkable.

### 9. "Peter was from the tribe of Judah" (Part 2) — **Not in the text**

**Claim**: Peter "was from the tribe of Judah."

**Finding**: The New Testament says Peter was from Bethsaida (John 1:44) and lived in Capernaum (Mark 1:21, 29) — both in Galilee, not Judea. His tribal lineage is not stated in scripture. By the 1st century CE the term *Yehudim*/Ἰουδαῖοι ("Jews") referred to ethnic Jews generally rather than members of the literal tribe of Judah; only Levi (priestly) and possibly Benjamin (Paul, Phil. 3:5) had reliably preserved tribal identity.

### 10. "Peter spoke Hebrew, did not speak Greek" (Part 2) — **Probably mostly Aramaic; partially overstated**

**Claim**: Peter spoke Hebrew, not Greek; Mark was needed as his interpreter.

**Finding**: First-century Galilean Jews most commonly spoke **Aramaic** (the everyday vernacular), with Hebrew preserved as a religious/literary language and Greek widely present (Galilee bordered the Decapolis). The Mark-as-interpreter tradition (Papias c. 110, Irenaeus c. 180) is real, but Papias' word *hermēneutēs* could mean either "translator" or "transmitter/expounder of teaching." The blanket claim that Peter "did not speak Greek" goes beyond the evidence — most scholars now think 1st-century Galilean Jews had at least functional Greek for commerce. The video's broader point (that "Christianos" is a Greek-cultural framing imposed on a Hebrew/Aramaic Messianic community) doesn't depend on Peter being monolingual.

**Source**: standard treatments in Bauckham's *Jesus and the Eyewitnesses* and the *Cambridge History of Judaism* vol. 3.

### 11. "Library of Alexandria was burned down by the Romans" (Part 13) — **Contested; multiple events, not one Roman act**

**Claim**: The Library of Alexandria was "burned down by the Romans."

**Finding**: There are at least four candidate destruction events: (1) Caesar's accidental fire during the Alexandrian War, 48 BCE; (2) Aurelian's siege, c. 273 CE; (3) Christian destruction of the Serapeum/its annex, 391 CE under Theodosius; (4) the Muslim conquest, 642 CE. Most modern historians think the library declined gradually rather than burning in one spectacular incident. Crediting "the Romans" as a single agent is an oversimplification.

**Source**: [Library of Alexandria — Britannica](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Library-of-Alexandria)

### 12. Constantine's Sunday law and the timing of Sunday observance (Parts 10, 15) — **Mostly correct, with one nuance**

**Claim**: Constantine instituted Sunday observance and called it the "Lord's Day" / day of the sun.

**Finding**: Largely accurate as to Constantine. His **3 March 321 CE** law called Sunday "the venerable day of the Sun" (*venerabili die Solis*) and required rest from work for urban professions. **However**: Sunday observance among Christians is attested *before* Constantine — Justin Martyr's *First Apology* 67 (c. 155 CE) describes Sunday assembly, and the *Didache* (late 1st / early 2nd century) and Ignatius (c. 110) reference it. The video itself acknowledges Justin Martyr (correctly) in Part 15. So the precise claim "Constantine made Christians worship on Sunday" would be wrong; the more nuanced claim ("Constantine *codified* it for the empire") is right.

**Source**: [Codex Justinianus 3.12.2 (Constantine's Sunday law)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sabbath_in_Christianity#Constantine's_legislation_(321_CE))

### 13. Council of Nicaea separating Easter from Passover (Part 10) — **Substantially correct**

**Claim**: Nicaea forbade Christians from celebrating Easter at the same time as the Jewish Passover.

**Finding**: Correct in substance. Constantine's letter to the bishops after Nicaea (preserved by Eusebius, *Vita Constantini* 3.18–19) explicitly says Christians should not be "in common with the Jews" in observing Easter, and the Quartodeciman position (which kept Passover-aligned dates) was suppressed. The Nicene canons themselves don't specify the Easter rule, but the post-conciliar correspondence does.

**Source**: [Quartodecimanism — Britannica](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Quartodecimanism)

### 14. Justin Martyr quotes (Parts 8, 15) — **Quoted accurately**

**Claim**: Justin Martyr's *First Apology* 67 and *Dialogue with Trypho* (various chapters) defend Sunday worship and reject Sabbath observance.

**Finding**: Quotes are accurate. *Dialogue with Trypho* and *First Apology* are extant; the passages cited match standard translations (Roberts/Donaldson). The speaker's interpretation that Justin's reasoning is supersessionist is also defensible — Justin explicitly identifies Christians as the "true spiritual Israel" (*Dial.* 11.5) and says Sabbath was given to Israel "because of their unrighteousness" (*Dial.* 18, 21).

**Source**: [*Dialogue with Trypho* — newadvent.org](https://www.newadvent.org/fathers/01287.htm)

### 15. Hanukkah / Maccabean dates (Part 14) — **Substantially correct**

**Claim**: Antiochus IV's persecution; Mattathias and Judah Maccabee; rededication of the Temple on 25 Kislev 165 BCE; Hanukkah celebrated for 8 days.

**Finding**: The traditional dating of the rededication is **December 14, 164 BCE** (25 Kislev), not 165, but some sources do place the rededication in 165 BCE depending on how the Seleucid era is anchored; this is a one-year scholarly disagreement, not a factual error of substance. Everything else (Antiochus's Hellenizing decrees, the Tobiads, the desecration with a statue of Zeus on the altar, the eight-day rededication) tracks the account in 1 Maccabees and Josephus' *Antiquities* book 12.

**Source**: 1 Maccabees 4:36–59; Josephus, *Antiquities* 12.7.

### 16. The Edomite identification of Herod Agrippa II (Part 6) — **Half-right**

**Claim**: "Herod Agrippa II, the Edomite ruler of Judea."

**Finding**: The Herodian dynasty was Idumaean (descended from converts to Judaism after the Hasmonean conquest of Idumaea c. 125 BCE). Idumaea is the Greek name for Edom, so calling Herod "Edomite" is etymologically defensible but anachronistic in a 1st-century context — the family had been Jewish by religion for ~150 years by Agrippa II's time. Josephus (*Antiquities* 14, 15) uses both terms.

## Things I deliberately did NOT fact-check

These are theological/interpretive positions where the speaker is taking a side in a long debate. They are not "false claims" in the way the items above are; they are minority or sectarian positions that mainstream Christians, Messianic Jews, and academic biblical scholars would reject for *different* reasons:

- The Trinity is unbiblical / Jesus is not God
- The Sabbath must be Saturday and Sunday observance is sin
- The name "Jesus" is Hellenized paganism and only "Yahushua" / "Yahuwah" are valid
- Paul is misused but not rejected (the speaker's specific middle position; Hebrew Israelites usually reject Paul outright, mainstream Christians usually treat him as authoritative)
- The New Covenant excludes "Christians" as a category and applies only to ethnic Israel + grafted-in Gentiles
- The promised end-times gathering is a physical regathering of scattered Israel, not a spiritual rapture
- The eternal kingdom is on earth, not heaven
- The African diaspora is biblical Israel under the curses of Deuteronomy 28

The last claim in particular — Black Hebrew Israelite identity theology — is rejected by mainstream genetics, historiography, and biblical scholarship, but the question of who counts as "Israel" today is fundamentally a religious-identity claim and arguments against it are unlikely to be persuasive on factual grounds alone. The series itself (Part 5) acknowledges this is a contested identity.
