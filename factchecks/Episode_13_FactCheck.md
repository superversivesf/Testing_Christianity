# Episode 13 Fact-Check — Alexander the Great and Hellenization

## Framing Note

As with the series-wide fact-check policy, we adjudicate **dated historical claims, document attributions, etymologies, and claims about scholarly consensus**. We do **not** adjudicate theological/interpretive claims (whether Greek culture is "satanic," whether Christianity is a "religion of tares," or whether Hebrew is the "language of creation").

---

## Verified / Substantially Correct Claims

### 1. Alexander studied under Aristotle
**Claim**: "Alexander, he was a student of Aristotle."

**Finding**: Correct. Aristotle was invited to the Macedonian court by Philip II around 343 BCE and tutored the young Alexander. The relationship is attested in multiple ancient sources, including Plutarch's *Life of Alexander*.

**Source**: [Aristotle — Britannica](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Aristotle)

### 2. Alexander died in 323 BCE at age 33
**Claim**: "In 323 BC, Alexander came down with a fever, and within a short time, he died. He was 33 years old."

**Finding**: Correct. Alexander died in Babylon in June 323 BCE, likely age 32 (by modern counting; "33" is a common rounded figure). Accounts vary between fever, poison, or other illness, but the date and approximate age are standard.

**Source**: [Alexander the Great — Britannica](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Alexander-the-Great)

### 3. Ptolemy seized Alexander's corpse
**Claim**: "Ptolemy... hijacked Alexander's corpse and used it as proof of his succession and seized Egypt."

**Finding**: Correct in substance. The *Alexandrian Wars* tradition (Diodorus Siculus 18.28, Pausanias 1.6.3, Curtius Rufus 10.10.20) records that Ptolemy intercepted the funeral cortege transporting Alexander's body from Babylon to Macedonia and redirected it to Egypt, where it was eventually interred in Alexandria. Possession of the body was used to bolster Ptolemaic legitimacy.

**Source**: [Ptolemy I Soter — Britannica](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Ptolemy-I-Soter)

### 4. Alexander was recognized as pharaoh and associated with divine kingship
**Claim**: Egypt "placed Alexander on the throne of the pharaohs, giving him the crown of Upper and Lower Egypt, and named him the incarnation of Ra and Osiris."

**Finding**: Correct in substance. Alexander was crowned pharaoh at Memphis in 332 BCE. Egyptian temple reliefs and the Alexander Romance tradition identify him with Amun-Ra. The specific pairing with Osiris is less prominent in primary Egyptian sources but is not anachronistic—Egyptian pharaohs were regularly identified with Horus and, after death, with Osiris. The broader claim that he was fully integrated into Egyptian kingship ideology is accurate.

**Source**: [Alexander in Egypt — Livius.org](https://www.livius.org/articles/person/alexander-the-great/alexander-in-egypt/)

### 5. The diadochi kingdoms were monarchies, not democracies
**Claim**: "One ideal that they did not spread, since it would undermine their power, was Athenian democracy."

**Finding**: Correct. The Hellenistic kingdoms (Macedonian Antigonids, Seleucids, Ptolemies) were hereditary monarchies. While Greek *poleis* retained some local civic institutions, the diadochi did not export Athenian-style democracy to their subject populations.

**Source**: [Hellenistic Age — Britannica](https://www.britannica.com/event/Hellenistic-Age)

### 6. Rome adopted and spread Greek culture
**Claim**: "Rome basically conquered the Greeks, but took their culture as their own... Roman architecture, art, literature, and religion... showed influence by the Greeks."

**Finding**: Correct. Roman cultural "Hellenization" is a major theme of classical historiography. Rome looted Greek art (e.g., after the sack of Corinth, 146 BCE), imported Greek tutors, and adopted Greek architectural orders, religious syncretism, and literary models. The speaker's hyperbole ("every piece of Greek art") is rhetorical, but the core claim is sound.

**Source**: [Roman art and architecture — Britannica](https://www.britannica.com/art/Roman-art)

### 7. The word "diploma" derives from Greek
**Claim**: "The word diploma comes from the Greek. It means double-folded paper."

**Finding**: Correct. Greek *δίπλωμα* (diplōma), from *διπλόω* (diplóō, "to fold double"), meant a folded document or official letter of recommendation. The "double-folded" gloss is etymologically accurate.

**Source**: [diploma — Wiktionary](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/diploma)

## Claims Requiring Correction or Nuance

### 8. "Pentateuch means the 70" / "70 scribes translated the Pentateuch — that's why it's called the Septuagint"
**Claim**: "[The translation] is called the Pentateuch which means the 70 and because 70 learned scribes were a part of this translation the Pentateuch was called the Septuagint."

**Finding**: Two different Greek-derived terms have been conflated into a single false etymology.

| Term | Etymology | Meaning |
|------|-----------|---------|
| **Pentateuch** | Greek *pentáteukhos* (πεντάτευχος) | "Five scrolls/books" — the first five books of the Bible (Genesis–Deuteronomy). Has **nothing** to do with the number 70. |
| **Septuagint** | Latin *septuaginta* ("seventy") | The Greek translation of the Hebrew scriptures. The *Letter of Aristeas* legend says **72** translators (six from each tribe) produced the translation under Ptolemy II; the name "Septuagint/seventy" rounds 72 down. The legend originally applied only to the Pentateuch and was later extended to the entire Greek Old Testament. |

The speaker correctly associates "70 scribes" with the Septuagint legend, but he incorrectly says "Pentateuch means the 70." This appears to be a spoken slip or confusion—he seems to be trying to say the *translation* was called the Septuagint because of the 70/72 translators, but he garbles it by attributing the "70" meaning to "Pentateuch" itself.

**Sources**:
- [Septuagint — Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Septuagint)
- [Letter of Aristeas — Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Letter_of_Aristeas)

### 9. "The Library of Alexandria was burned down by the Romans"
**Claim**: "Alexandria on the Egyptian coast would eventually become the home of the ancient world's greatest library until it was burned down by the Romans."

**Finding**: Oversimplified and misleading. The Library did not burn in a single Roman act. Modern scholarship identifies at least four candidate destruction events and generally supports a model of **gradual decline** rather than catastrophic annihilation:

1. **Caesar's Alexandrian War, 48 BCE** — Caesar's soldiers set fire to ships; the blaze spread and may have damaged an annex or warehouse near the docks. Ancient sources give conflicting reports on the extent of damage (Seneca says 40,000 scrolls; Dio says dockyard storehouses). The main Library seems to have survived or been rebuilt.
2. **Aurelian's siege, c. 273 CE** — Fighting between Palmyrene and Roman forces destroyed the Brucheion quarter, where the main Library was located.
3. **The Serapeum, 391 CE** — The Christian bishop Theophilus obtained a decree from Theodosius to demolish pagan temples; the Serapeum (which housed a "daughter library") was destroyed. This was done under Roman/Christian imperial authority.
4. **Arab conquest, 642 CE** — Arabic sources (al-Qiftī, Bar-Hebraeus) claim Caliph Umar ordered the books burned. Most modern scholars reject this story as a later myth; the Library was likely long gone by then.

The speaker's summary crediting "the Romans" as the singular agent who "burned down" the Library is inaccurate. If he means Caesar or Aurelian, those were specific events centuries apart, not a unified Roman policy.

**Sources**:
- [Library of Alexandria — Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Library_of_Alexandria)
- [Library of Alexandria — Britannica](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Library-of-Alexandria)

### 10. "Josephus said Jews did not have a culture of learning other languages"
**Claim**: "Josephus himself... said that the Yahudim did not have a culture of learning the other languages of the world. It was something that they frowned upon."

**Finding**: Overstated. Josephus does say that Greek learning was not cultivated among Jews to the same degree as their own traditions, but he does **not** say Jews categorically refused or frowned upon learning other languages.

- In *Antiquities* 20.263–264, Josephus notes that he himself labored to attain Greek literary skill and that "our people do not welcome those who have mastered the tongue of many nations." But he is discussing *rhetorical polish and literary eloquence*, not practical multilingualism.
- Josephus wrote *The Jewish War* first in his "native tongue" (Aramaic/Hebrew) and then translated it into Greek, showing he expected a Greek-reading audience of Jews.
- First-century Judea and Galilee were multilingual environments: Aramaic (vernacular), Hebrew (liturgical), and Greek (commerce, administration, diaspora) all coexisted. Archaeological evidence (inscriptions, papyri) confirms widespread Greek use even in Judea.

The speaker's blanket claim that Jews "frowned upon" learning other languages is too strong; Josephus' statement is more nuanced and concerns literary attainment, not practical language learning.

**Sources**:
- Josephus, *Antiquities* 20.263–264
- [Josephus — Britannica](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Flavius-Josephus)

### 11. "The origin of soccer itself can be traced to ancient Greece"
**Claim**: "The origin of soccer itself, the football, what they call it around the world, its origins can be traced to ancient Greece."

**Finding**: Highly misleading. The speaker conflates the ancient Greek value of *agon* (competition) and Greek ball games (episkyros, phaininda) with the modern sport of association football (soccer). While the Greeks did play ball games, **modern soccer was codified in England in 1863** by the Football Association, with rules derived from medieval English public-school football games, not from ancient Greek prototypes. FIFA cites *cuju* in Han-dynasty China as the earliest evidence of an organized kicking game.

There is no continuous lineage from ancient Greek ball games to modern soccer. This claim is an example of retroactive cultural genealogy—finding superficial similarities and asserting direct descent.

**Sources**:
- [History of association football — Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History_of_association_football)
- [Football — Britannica](https://www.britannica.com/sports/football-soccer)

### 12. "Hezekiah the high priest" accompanied Ptolemy to Egypt in 311 BCE
**Claim**: "In 311 BC, the high priest Hezekiah went with Ptolemy to Egypt."

**Finding**: Partially correct but misleadingly titled. The reference is to Josephus, *Against Apion* 1.186–189 / *Antiquities* 12.2–3. Josephus cites Hecataeus of Abdera describing a Jewish leader named Hezekiah (*Ezekias*) who led a large group of Jews to Egypt under Ptolemy I. However, Josephus/Hecataeus describes him as a man of high standing and learning, and possibly a priest, but **not necessarily the reigning High Priest** (*kohen gadol*) of Jerusalem at that time.

The Oniad high-priestly line is well documented; there is no "High Priest Hezekiah" in the standard chronology of Zadokite high priests around 311 BCE. The speaker upgrades "a priestly leader named Hezekiah" to "the high priest Hezekiah," which is an overstatement.

**Sources**:
- Josephus, *Against Apion* 1.186–189
- Josephus, *Antiquities* 12.2–3
- [Oniad high priests — Jewish Encyclopedia](https://www.jewishencyclopedia.com/articles/11842-onias)

### 13. Hellenistic Age "from 323 BC to 330 AD"
**Claim**: The speaker quotes Britannica saying the Hellenistic Age spanned from 323 BCE "all the way to 330 AD, basically spanning almost 600 years."

**Finding**: The Britannica quote is **genuine but truncated**. The full Britannica entry gives the standard range (323 BCE – 30 BCE) and then adds: "For some purposes, the period is extended for a further three and a half centuries to the move by Constantine the Great... in 330 CE." The speaker omits the qualifier "For some purposes" and treats the extended range as the definitive dates. Most historians use 323–31 BCE (Battle of Actium) or 30 BCE (death of Cleopatra); the 330 CE endpoint is a maximalist, pedagogical convention, not consensus.

This is a minor point, but the omission of "for some purposes" gives the impression that all historians accept a ~650-year Hellenistic Age, which is not true.

**Source**: [Hellenistic Age — Britannica](https://www.britannica.com/event/Hellenistic-Age)

### 14. The speaker's definition of "Hellenization"
**Claim**: "Hellenization is the adoption of Greek culture, religion, language, and identity by non-Greeks."

**Finding**: Correct as a general definition. This is the standard scholarly usage. The speaker adds theological framing ("Satan's strategy"), but the basic definition is accurate.

**Source**: [Hellenization — Oxford Classical Dictionary](https://oxfordre.com/classics/view/10.1093/acrefore/9780199381135.001.0001/acrefore-9780199381135-e-2966)

## Theological Claims (Out of Scope)

The following are interpretive/theological claims that fall outside the scope of factual adjudication, per the project framing note:

- Greek culture is "the culture of Satan."
- Hellenization is Satan's strategy to reverse Babel.
- Christianity is a modern continuation of Hellenization.
- Hebrew is "the language of creation" (citing Jubilees 12:25–28).
- The Book of Jubilees was excluded from the Bible specifically to hide Hebrew's primacy.
- The New Testament was originally written in Hebrew, not Greek.
- Greek philosophy (logic/reasoning) is the source of Christian doctrinal error.

These are theological positions that mainstream Christianity, Judaism, and biblical scholarship would reject for different reasons; they are not verifiable historical facts and are therefore not scored as "true" or "false" in this document.
