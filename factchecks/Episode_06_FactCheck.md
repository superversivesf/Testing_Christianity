# Episode 6 — Fact-Check: What Happened After the Book of Acts

**Source:** Testing Christianity Pt. 6 ("Truth Unedited" / sacred-name Hebrew-Israelite series)  
**Transcript file:** `transcripts/06 - TESTING_CHRISTIANITY_PT._6_WHAT_HAPPENED_AFTER_THE_BOOK_OF_ACTS.txt`

**Scope:** This document checks dated historical claims, document attributions, and named historical events. It does **not** adjudicate theological claims (sacred names, covenant theology, identity of Israel, etc.), which are outside factual verification.

---

## Claim 1: Paul before Nero c. 62 AD

**Claim:** Paul made it to Rome to plead his case to Caesar (Nero) around 62 AD.

**Verdict:** **TRUE** (within scholarly consensus)

**Reasoning:**
- Acts 28 records Paul’s arrival in Rome and his house arrest. The traditional dating places this in the early 60s CE.
- Nero reigned 54–68 CE. The majority of scholars date Paul’s Roman imprisonment to c. 60–62 CE.
- The Book of Acts ends before a verdict, consistent with the speaker’s note that what happens after is less biblically detailed.

**Sources:**
- [Book of Acts — BibleHub (Acts 28)](https://biblehub.com/acts/28.htm)
- [New Testament chronology — Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chronology_of_the_Apostle_Paul)

## Claim 2: The Roman Empire controlled the known world in 62 AD

**Claim:** "Much of the known world was under the control of the Roman government."

**Verdict:** **TRUE**

**Reasoning:**
- At its height under Trajan (later than 62 CE), Rome controlled from Britain to Mesopotamia, from the Rhine/Danube to North Africa. In 62 CE the empire encompassed the entire Mediterranean basin, Gaul, Britain (since 43 CE), the Balkans, Anatolia, the Levant, Egypt, and North Africa. It was the dominant power of the "known world" from a Mediterranean perspective.

**Sources:**
- [Roman Empire — Britannica](https://www.britannica.com/place/Roman-Empire)

## Claim 3: The land was called Judea, not Palestine, in 62 AD

**Claim:** "Many people today refer to it as Palestine, but it was not Palestine at that time."

**Verdict:** **TRUE** (with minor nuance)

**Reasoning:**
- The Roman province was officially named **Judaea** (Latin: *Iudaea*) after the Hasmonean kingdom and Herodian tetrarchies were absorbed.
- The term "Palestina" (from Latin *Palaestina*, derived from Greek *Philistia*) was applied to the broader region by Herodotus in the 5th century BCE but did not become the official *provincial* name until after the Bar Kokhba revolt (see Claim 11 below).
- So in 62 CE the province was indeed called Judaea, not Syria Palaestina.

**Sources:**
- [Judaea (Roman province) — Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Judaea_(Roman_province))

## Claim 4: The Temple still stood in 62 AD and Jews used it for sacrifices

**Claim:** "At this period in 62 AD, the Yahudim still had their temple. And many who rejected Yahusha as the Messiah, they were still using the temple for animal sacrifices for atonement."

**Verdict:** **TRUE**

**Reasoning:**
- The Second Temple stood until its destruction in 70 CE. Daily sacrifices (Tamid) continued until the siege disrupted them in 70 CE.
- The speaker’s description of mainstream Jews continuing to use the Temple for sacrificial atonement matches Josephus and the New Testament.

**Sources:**
- [Second Temple — Britannica](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Second-Temple)
- Josephus, *The Jewish War*, books 5–6.

## Claim 5: Paul went to synagogues, not pagan temples

**Claim:** "This is why when Paul was spreading the message of Messiah, he was not going to the pagan temples. He went to the synagogues."

**Verdict:** **TRUE** (substantially)

**Reasoning:**
- Acts consistently describes Paul first visiting local synagogues in each city (e.g., Acts 13:14; 14:1; 17:1–2; 18:4).
- While he also preached in other venues (Areopagus in Athens, a riverside prayer place in Philippi, the hall of Tyrannus in Ephesus), his consistent *starting point* was the synagogue, because he targeted Jews and God-fearing Gentiles attached to Jewish communities.

**Sources:**
- [Acts 13, 17, 18 — BibleHub](https://biblehub.com/acts/13.htm)
- [Paul’s missionary strategy — Bible Odyssey](https://www.bibleodyssey.org/people/related-articles/pauls-missionary-strategy.aspx)

## Claim 6: The label "Christian" began as derogatory and was not used by the apostles for themselves

**Claim:** "The apostles never called themselves this term. They called themselves disciples, and they say they followed a way. The term Christians was derogatory..."

**Verdict:** **MIXED**

**Reasoning:**
- Acts 11:26 says the disciples were **first called Christians** (*Christianoi*) at Antioch; the passive voice suggests it was a label applied from outside.
- Acts 26:28 has Agrippa using it, again as an external label.
- 1 Peter 4:16 uses the term, though some scholars debate whether it is an interpolation or reflects a later stage.
- The speaker is correct that "disciples" and "followers of the Way" (Acts 9:2; 19:9, 23; 22:4; 24:14, 22) are the terms used *internally* in Acts.
- Calling it "derogatory" is plausible but not certain. The term was likely coined by outsiders, possibly with mockery (the suffix *-ianos* was often used for partisan followers of a figure), but it may not have been a slur in the same sense as later insults.

**Sources:**
- [Acts 11:26 — BibleHub](https://biblehub.com/acts/11-26.htm)
- [1 Peter 4:16 — BibleHub](https://biblehub.com/1_peter/4-16.htm)
- [*Christianos* — Etymonline](https://www.etymonline.com/word/Christian)

## Claim 7: Nero persecuted the believers; Peter and Paul were executed under him

**Claim:** "Peter and Paul, they were both said to be executed under Nero."

**Verdict:** **SCHOLARLY DEBATE** (but broadly accepted by many scholars)

**Reasoning:**
- Early Christian tradition (1 Clement 5, c. 96 CE; Tertullian; Eusebius, *EH* 2.25) holds that Peter was crucified and Paul beheaded under Nero.
- Eusebius specifically says Paul was beheaded and Peter crucified upside down (citing Origen and earlier tradition).
- The actual *primary* evidence is thin. The New Testament does not record their deaths. But the early Christian consensus is strong enough that most scholars accept the tradition as historically plausible, even if not independently verifiable.
- Some scholars regard the details (especially the upside-down crucifixion) as pious elaboration.

**Sources:**
- [Eusebius, *Church History* 2.25 — New Advent](https://www.newadvent.org/fathers/250102.htm)
- [1 Clement 5 — Early Christian Writings](http://www.earlychristianwritings.com/text/1clement-roberts.html)
- [Nero’s persecution of Christians — Britannica](https://www.britannica.com/event/Great-Fire-of-Rome)

## Claim 8: Paul’s final letter to Timothy (2 Timothy) was written with death imminent

**Claim:** "He wrote his final letter to Timothy... In the letter, he did express his final thoughts, just in case Timothy did not arrive in time. We do not know whether Timothy came."

**Verdict:** **TRUE** (as a description of the epistolary tradition)

**Reasoning:**
- 2 Timothy is widely regarded as Paul’s final extant letter, written during a Roman imprisonment when he expected execution.
- 2 Tim 4:6–8: "I am already being poured out as a drink offering... the time of my departure has come."
- We do not have any record of whether Timothy arrived before Paul’s death.

**Sources:**
- [2 Timothy 4:6–8 — BibleHub](https://biblehub.com/2_timothy/4.htm)
- [Pastoral Epistles — New Testament Introduction](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Pastoral-Epistles)

## Claim 9: Josephus originally wrote *The Jewish War* in Hebrew

**Claim:** "Josephus, whose original name was Yosef ben Matayahu, this work was said to be originally written in Hebrew so that the Yahudim in other regions could read it. Josephus, though, later translated it into Greek with the help of others."

**Verdict:** **MIXED / PARTIALLY TRUE** (the speaker seems to conflate *Jewish War* with *Antiquities*)

**Reasoning:**
- Josephus says (*Jewish War* 1.3) that he first composed the work "in the language of my country" for the Parthian Jews, then prepared a Greek version. Most scholars understand this to mean **Aramaic** (or possibly a literary Hebrew/Aramaic hybrid), not modern Hebrew per se.
- *Antiquities of the Jews* (completed c. 93/94 CE), by contrast, was composed **directly in Greek** for a Greco-Roman audience.
- The speaker appears to conflate the two works when he says "Antiquities of the Jews and the War of the Jews, both by Josephus... this work was said to be originally written in Hebrew." The claim about a Hebrew original applies to *Jewish War*, not *Antiquities*.

**Sources:**
- Josephus, *Jewish War* 1.3 — referenced in [Flavius Josephus — Britannica](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Flavius-Josephus)
- [Flavius Josephus — Britannica](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Flavius-Josephus)

## Claim 10: Not one stone of the Temple building was left standing after the war of 70 AD

**Claim:** "Not one stone of the building was left standing after the war of 70 AD."

**Verdict:** **MIXED**

**Reasoning:**
- Jesus’ prophecy in Matthew 24:2 said "not one stone shall be left upon another."
- Josephus describes the Temple’s destruction, but also the sparing of some towers (Phasael, Hippicus, Mariamne) and parts of the western wall to serve as a Roman garrison.
- The **Temple building itself** was destroyed, but a **section of the retaining wall** (the Western Wall) remained, and some towers survived.
- The speaker quotes the prophecy and the widespread destruction accurately, but the absolute claim "not one stone" is hyperbolic if taken literally for the entire Temple Mount complex.
- The Romans did, according to Josephus, pry apart stonework to retrieve melted gold, fulfilling the sense that the structures were systematically dismantled.

**Sources:**
- [Siege of Jerusalem (70 CE) — Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Siege_of_Jerusalem_(70_CE))
- Josephus, *Jewish War* 6.271, 7.1–2

## Claim 11: The Temple was burned on August 28, 70 AD, and the last sacrifice on August 6

**Claim:** "The last animal sacrifice was offered on the great altar of the temple on August 6. The temple was burned on August 28, 70 AD."

**Verdict:** **SCHOLARLY DEBATE / UNVERIFIABLE** (specific dates are traditional, not independently attested)

**Reasoning:**
- Josephus says the Temple was destroyed on the **10th of Av** (or the 9th, depending on rabbinic vs. Josephus chronology) in the year 70 CE.
- Converting to the Gregorian calendar depends on lunar calculations. Many sources place the destruction on **August 10** (Julian calendar) or approximately **late July/early August**.
- The exact dates "August 6" and "August 28" are **not directly attested in primary sources** and appear to be from secondary reckonings or perhaps the speaker’s edition of an encyclopedia.
- The month is correct (Av = July/August), but the specific day numbers should be treated as approximations or traditional claims rather than independently verified facts.

**Sources:**
- [Tisha B'Av — Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tisha_B%27Av)
- Josephus, *Jewish War* 6.250

## Claim 12: Gessius Florus was Roman governor in 64 AD and was corrupt/cruel

**Claim:** "The Roman governor appointed over Judea in 64 AD was Gessius Florus. He was said to be a corrupt and cruel man."

**Verdict:** **TRUE**

**Reasoning:**
- Gessius Florus was indeed procurator of Judea c. 64–66 CE.
- Tacitus calls him unfit for office; Josephus portrays him as plundering the region.
- His raid on the Temple treasury is a well-documented precipitating event for the revolt.

**Sources:**
- [Gessius Florus — Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gessius_Florus)
- Josephus, *Jewish War* 2.277–279

## Claim 13: Herod Agrippa II was an "Edomite ruler of Judea" and urged peace

**Claim:** "Herod Agrippa II, the Edomite ruler of Judea... he was the one that Paul appeared before... He urged the Yahudim to be at peace, but he was rejected. He stayed loyal to Rome, not the Yahudim."

**Verdict:** **MIXED**

**Reasoning:**
- Agrippa II did appear before Paul (Acts 25–26) and urged the Jews to remain loyal to Rome.
- However, he was **not** the "ruler of Judea" in the sense of governor. He ruled territories **outside** direct Judea (Chalcis, then Batanaea, Trachonitis, etc.) and had oversight of the Temple.
- The Herodian dynasty was **Idumaean** (Greek for Edom), descended from converts to Judaism. Calling Agrippa II "Edomite" is etymologically defensible but anachronistic — by his time the family had been Jewish by religion for ~150 years.
- He was indeed loyal to Rome.

**Sources:**
- [Herod Agrippa II — Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Herod_Agrippa_II)
- [Acts 25–26 — BibleHub](https://biblehub.com/acts/25.htm)

## Claim 14: Menahem captured Masada, then Jerusalem; he was killed in 66 AD

**Claim:** "A rebel leader named Menahem led a band of men to capture the Roman military fortress at Masada... Then... went on to capture the Roman garrison in Jerusalem next to the temple. Menahem was killed in 66 AD."

**Verdict:** **TRUE**

**Reasoning:**
- Menahem ben Judah (or son of Judas the Galilean, per Josephus) did lead Sicarii to capture Masada and then participated in the siege of Jerusalem.
- He was captured, tortured, and executed by Eleazar ben Hananiah’s faction in late 66 CE.

**Sources:**
- Josephus, *Jewish War* 2.433–448

## Claim 15: Vespasian resumed his offensive in June 69 AD

**Claim:** "Vespasian resumed his military offensive of Judea in June of 69 AD."

**Verdict:** **TRUE** (broadly)

**Reasoning:**
- After Nero’s death in June 68, Vespasian paused operations in Judea due to the Year of the Four Emperors.
- He was proclaimed emperor by the legions in the East in July 69 CE and soon departed for Rome.
- The timeline of resuming offensive operations in **June 69** is plausible, though the exact month is a matter of reconstruction.

**Sources:**
- [Year of the Four Emperors — Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Year_of_the_Four_Emperors)

## Claim 16: Hadrian renamed the province Syria-Palestina and Jerusalem Aelia Capitolina

**Claim:** "Emperor Hadrian wiped the province of Judea off the Roman map, renaming it Syria-Palestina. He renamed Jerusalem Aelia Capitolina after himself..."

**Verdict:** **TRUE** (with minor nuance on timing)

**Reasoning:**
- After the Bar Kokhba revolt (132–136 CE), Hadrian did rename the province **Syria Palaestina** (or Syria Palestina).
- He renamed the rebuilt Jerusalem **Aelia Capitolina** (after his family name *Aelius* and Jupiter Capitolinus).
- Some scholars debate whether the renaming and refounding happened before the revolt (as a trigger) or after (as punishment), or both.
- The discovery of Aelia Capitolina coins hoarded *before* the revolt ended shows the colony was founded and minting *before* 135 CE, supporting the view that the renaming was at least partly a cause of the revolt.

**Sources:**
- [Bar Kokhba Revolt — Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bar_Kokhba_revolt)
- [Aelia Capitolina — Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aelia_Capitolina)
- [Syria Palaestina — Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Syria_Palaestina)

## Claim 17: Bar Kokhba led the revolt against Hadrian after circumcision ban

**Claim:** "He placed many restrictions on their way of life, banning circumcisions. This led to a revolt by the Hebrews led by Simon Bar Kokhba."

**Verdict:** **SCHOLARLY DEBATE**

**Reasoning:**
- The *Historia Augusta* says the Jews revolted because they were forbidden to practice circumcision.
- Cassius Dio attributes the revolt primarily to Hadrian’s establishment of Aelia Capitolina.
- Modern scholars are divided: some accept a pre-revolt ban on circumcision (Smallwood); others argue the ban was imposed as punishment *after* the revolt (Oppenheimer, Schäfer).
- Either way, the broader claim that Hadrian’s harsh policies triggered the Bar Kokhba revolt is mainstream.

**Sources:**
- [Bar Kokhba Revolt — Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bar_Kokhba_revolt)
- [Cassius Dio, Roman History 69.12–14](https://penelope.uchicago.edu/Thayer/E/Roman/Texts/Cassius_Dio/69*.html)
- [Historia Augusta, *Hadrian* 14.2](https://penelope.uchicago.edu/Thayer/E/Roman/Texts/Historia_Augusta/Hadrian/1*.html)

## Claim 18: Hebrews were banned from Jerusalem except one day a year (Tisha B'Av)

**Claim:** "The Hebrews were not even allowed in the city except for one day a year at Tisha B'Av."

**Verdict:** **SCHOLARLY DEBATE**

**Reasoning:**
- Rabbinic sources (e.g., *Lamentations Rabbah*) say Jews were allowed to enter Jerusalem once a year to mourn the Temple’s destruction, and the location was likely a designated spot.
- However, the evidence for this being a formal, empire-wide legal statute is not strong in non-rabbinic sources.
- The general point that Jews were largely excluded from Jerusalem after 135 CE is accepted; the specific "one day a year" detail comes from rabbinic memory and may reflect custom as much as statute.

**Sources:**
- [Aelia Capitolina — Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aelia_Capitolina)
- [Tisha B'Av — Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tisha_B%27Av)

## Claim 19: James (brother of Jesus) was stoned by the Jews

**Claim:** "James, the brother of Yahusha, who became the leader of the council at Jerusalem, he was stoned by the Yahudim."

**Verdict:** **SCHOLARLY DEBATE** (but widely accepted in some form)

**Reasoning:**
- Josephus (*Antiquities* 20.9.1) says James the brother of Jesus was tried by the Sanhedrin and stoned by order of the High Priest Ananus in c. 62 CE.
- Hegesippus (cited by Eusebius) gives a more elaborate Christian tradition that he was thrown down from the Temple pinnacle and then beaten to death.
- The basic claim that James was executed by Jewish authorities is supported by Josephus.

**Sources:**
- Josephus, *Antiquities* 20.9.1 — [Perseus/Tufts](http://www.perseus.tufts.edu/hopper/text?doc=Perseus:text:1999.01.0146:book=20:chapter=9)
- [James, brother of Jesus — Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/James,_brother_of_Jesus)

## Claim 20: After the apostles’ deaths, the faith was placed into the hands of the Gentiles

**Claim:** "It was after these deaths that the hands of the faith of the way were placed into the hands and the control of the Gentiles."

**Verdict:** **UNVERIFIABLE** (interpretive/theological framing)

**Reasoning:**
- This is a historical-theological framing, not a directly falsifiable fact.
- It is true that after the first generation of apostles died, Gentile believers became an ever-larger share of the movement, especially in the Roman cities.
- It is also true that leadership gradually shifted from Jerusalem-based Jewish apostles to Gentile bishops (e.g., in Rome, Antioch, Asia Minor).
- But the framing that this was a decisive or negative "handover" that caused "everything to take a turn" is interpretive and used by the speaker to set up his thesis about the origin of Christianity as a Gentile/Roman religion.
- We label it **UNVERIFIABLE** in the sense that the *moral/theological significance* the speaker attaches is not a matter of historical fact-checking.

## Claim 21: Josephus was a Yahudim Roman citizen

**Claim:** "He was a Yahudim Roman citizen who was known as a historian of his time."

**Verdict:** **TRUE**

**Reasoning:**
- Josephus (Yosef ben Matayahu) was a Jewish priest and military commander who defected to the Romans, was granted Roman citizenship, and became a client of the Flavian emperors.
- He wrote in Greek for a Roman audience.

**Sources:**
- [Flavius Josephus — Britannica](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Flavius-Josephus)
