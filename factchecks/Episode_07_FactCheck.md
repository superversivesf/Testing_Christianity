# Episode 7 — Fact-Check
## Testing Christianity: "The Unspoken History About the Birth of Christianity"

**Last updated:** 2026-04-29

**Scope note**: This document checks verifiable historical claims (dates, attributions, etymologies, scholarly consensus). It does **not** adjudicate theological claims (e.g., whether Christianity is a "religion of tares," whether the name "Jesus" is invalid, or whether Greek philosophy constitutes "empty deceit" per Col 2:8). Those are interpretive/theological positions on which scholars and believers disagree by definition.

## Claim 1: "There were people called Christians before belief in Jesus was known in Rome... there was already a pagan cult that worshiped and believed in... Serapis."

### What the video claims
At lines 34–36, the speaker asserts that there was "already a cult of people in Rome known as Christianos" before faith in Jesus arrived, worshipping Serapis, a Greco-Egyptian god.

### Verdict: **FALSE**

### Reasoning
The term "Christianos" (Χριστιανός) is well-attested as a neologism coined specifically for followers of Jesus. It first appears in the New Testament at **Acts 11:26**, where the disciples in Antioch are called "Christians," and is corroborated by the Roman historian **Tacitus** (c. 64–68 CE), who writes that the name "Christianos" came from "Christus" (*Annals* 15.44). There is **no independent documentary evidence** that any group calling themselves "Christianos" existed in Rome (or anywhere else) prior to the Jesus movement, nor that devotees of Serapis used that name for themselves.

The speaker is relying entirely on a single disputed document (see Claim 2 below).

### Sources
- [Tacitus, *Annals* 15.44](https://penelope.uchicago.edu/Thayer/E/Roman/Texts/Tacitus/Annals/15A*.html) — primary attestation of the term *Christianos* in a Roman historian
- [Acts 11:26 (Bible Hub / Greek interlinear)](https://biblehub.com/greek/christianoi_5546.htm)
- [Wikipedia — History of the term "Christian"](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History_of_the_term_Christian) — standard overview with primary references

## Claim 2: The Hadrian letter to Servianus saying Serapis-worshippers "are, in fact, Christians"

### What the video claims
At lines 82–102, the speaker reads a purported 134 CE letter from Emperor Hadrian to Servianus from the *Historia Augusta*, Vol. 3, pp. 399–406, including the line: "There those who worship Serapis are, in fact, Christians, and those who call themselves bishops of Christ are, in fact, devotees of Serapis."

### Verdict: **FALSE as a genuine Hadrianic document**

### Reasoning
The letter is found in the *Historia Augusta*, but modern classical scholarship overwhelmingly considers it a **late 4th-century forgery** (or at minimum a heavily fictionalized fabrication):

1. **Chronological impossibility**: Hadrian was in Egypt in 130 CE. Servianus was consul in 134 CE. Hadrian adopted Lucius Aelius Caesar in 136 CE. The letter references conditions that could not have existed simultaneously, making a single coherent date impossible.

2. **Anachronistic titles**: The reference to a "patriarch" of the Jewish synagogue fits the late 4th century (after the Bar Kokhba revolt, 132–136 CE, and the subsequent diaspora), not Hadrian's own reign.

3. **Authorship of the *Historia Augusta***: The work is now generally regarded as the product of a single author writing pseudonymously in the late 4th century (c. 390s CE), not a collection of independent biographies by multiple authors as it purports to be.

4. **Scholarly consensus**: The passage is widely treated by classicists as a fabrication. The World History Encyclopedia article the speaker himself cites (see Claim 3) includes a note that ancient writers "seem to have confused" the references, and that "Christians had nothing to do with the cult of Serapis."

Because the letter is a known forgery, it cannot serve as evidence that pagans were called "Christianos" before Jesus-followers existed.

### Sources
- [Historia Augusta — Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Historia_Augusta) — overview of authorship problems
- [Historia Augusta on UChicago/Penelope (Loeb), Life of Hadrian](https://penelope.uchicago.edu/Thayer/E/Roman/Texts/Historia_Augusta/Hadrian/1*.html) — primary text of the disputed biography
- [British Library / Historia Augusta resources](https://www.bl.uk/collection-items/historia-augusta-lives-of-the-later-caesars)
- [World History Encyclopedia — Serapis article](https://www.worldhistory.org/Serapis/) — notes the confusion and explicitly says Christians had nothing to do with Serapis

## Claim 3: Information read about Serapis from the World History Encyclopedia

### What the video claims
At lines 46–78, the speaker reads extensively from the World History Encyclopedia article on Serapis, including:
- Serapis was created by Ptolemy I Soter (r. 305–282 BCE)
- Blended Osiris, Apis, and Zeus
- Spread throughout the Roman Empire
- Banned by Theodosius I
- Popular in Rome by the 1st century CE
- Called a "redeemer god and savior"
- Cult ended with destruction of the Serapeum in Alexandria by Christians in 391–392 CE

### Verdict: **TRUE** (all major factual assertions match the source)

### Reasoning
The speaker's reading from the World History Encyclopedia (written by Joshua J. Mark, published 19 Oct 2021) is substantially accurate and faithful to the article. The dates, the description of the syncretic nature of the cult, the geographic spread, the role of Theodosius, and the description of Serapis as a "redeemer god and savior who granted believers eternal life" are all directly quoted or closely paraphrased from the source.

**Nuance**: While the encyclopedia article itself is popular rather than peer-reviewed academic, it is well-sourced and the factual statements cited are consistent with mainstream scholarship. The speaker's *interpretation* of the article (that Serapis worshippers were already "Christians") goes beyond what the article says — the article explicitly notes that ancient writers *confused* or *conflated* the two, and that Christians were not actually devotees of Serapis.

### Sources
- [World History Encyclopedia — Serapis](https://www.worldhistory.org/Serapis/) — article by Joshua J. Mark, 2021

## Claim 4: "In the advent of the Lord, philosophy was necessary to the Greeks for righteousness... as a pedagogue to bring the Hellenic mind to Christ, as the law did the Hebrews." (Clement of Alexandria quote)

### What the video claims
At lines 419–431, the speaker quotes Clement as saying Greek philosophy was a "pedagogue" (teacher) to bring the Greek mind to Christ, equivalent to the law bringing the Hebrews.

### Verdict: **TRUE (attribution correct; quote substantially accurate)**

### Reasoning
The speaker quotes from Clement's *Stromata* (Miscellanies). The quotations attributed to Clement are real passages and match the English translations found in the Ante-Nicene Fathers series (Volume 2, translated by William Wilson).

- The Greek philosophy-as-pedagogue theme is central to Clement's apologetic in *Stromata* Book 1 and 5. He explicitly writes: philosophy was a "kind of preparatory training to those who attain to the faith through demonstration... a pedagogue to bring the Hellenic mind to Christ, as the law did the Hebrews."
- Clement does write: "Philosophy is not, then, the product of vice, since it makes men virtuous. It follows, then, that it is the work of God." (also in *Stromata*)
- He also writes that philosophy does not comprehend the whole truth and lacks strength to perform the commandments, yet "prepares the way for the truly royal teaching."

**Nuance**: The speaker treats these as evidence Clement violated biblical commands against Gentile ways (Jer 10:2, Deut 12:29–32). Whether this constitutes a *theological error* is an interpretive/sectarian judgment outside the scope of fact-checking. But the speaker's claim that Clement held these views is factually correct.

### Sources
- [Clement of Alexandria, *Stromata* — Ante-Nicene Fathers Vol. 2](https://www.newadvent.org/fathers/0210.htm) — full text via New Advent
- [Wikipedia — Clement of Alexandria](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Clement_of_Alexandria) — biography confirming dates c. 150–215 CE and role at the Catechetical School

## Claim 5: Clement was head of the Catechetical School; his most famous pupil was Origen; Origen contributed to the Trinity and ransom theory of atonement.

### What the video claims
At lines 487–506, the speaker states: Clement founded the catechetical school in Alexandria; he taught Origen; Origen was a major contributor to the development of the Trinity and ransom theory of atonement and pioneered philosophical theology.

### Verdict: **MIXED / PARTIALLY TRUE**

### Reasoning
- **Clement at Alexandria**: TRUE. Clement taught at the Catechetical School of Alexandria. Whether he *founded* it or succeeded Pantaenus is debated by scholars (Eusebius is ambiguous), but he was a leading figure there by c. 190 CE.
- **Origen as Clement's pupil**: TRUE. Origen was a student of Clement and later succeeded him.
- **Origen contributed to Trinitarian theology**: TRUE in the sense that Origen articulated "eternal generation of the Son" and a three-hypostasis theology that later Cappadocian Fathers refined. However, modern scholars debate whether Origen's position was actually *subordinationist* — which is the opposite of orthodox Trinitarianism. He was **condemned posthumously** at the Second Council of Constantinople (553 CE) for views including subordinationism.
- **Origen "developed the Trinity" or was its "founder"**: FALSE as a sole-founding claim. The Trinity doctrine was a multi-century development involving Tertullian (who coined *trinitas*), Athanasius, the Cappadocian Fathers, and conciliar decisions at Nicaea (325) and Constantinople (381).
- **Ransom theory of atonement**: TRUE. Origen is widely credited as one of the major early proponents of the "ransom theory" (Christ's death as a ransom paid to Satan or to the powers of evil).

The speaker's claims are accurate in broad strokes but oversimplify the history of Trinitarian development by omitting Tertullian, Athanasius, and the Cappadocian Fathers.

### Sources
- [Origen — Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Origen) — overview of his complex relationship to later Trinitarian orthodoxy
- [Tertullian — Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tertullian) — originator of Latin Trinitarian vocabulary (*trinitas*, *persona*, *substantia*)
- [Cappadocian Fathers — Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cappadocian_Fathers) — Basil, Gregory of Nyssa, Gregory of Nazianzus who formalized Trinitarian theology

## Claim 6: The founding fathers of the U.S. studied Plato, Aristotle, and Epicurus.

### What the video claims
At lines 383–395, the speaker mentions the U.S. founding fathers studied Greek philosophers "like Aristotle, Plato, and Epicurus." He ties this to the "Masonic" founding fathers and fraternal societies.

### Verdict: **MIXED / MOSTLY TRUE for the general claim, stretching on specifics**

### Reasoning
- The influence of classical (Greek and Roman) political philosophy on the American founding is a well-documented subject. The founders widely read Greek authors; Jefferson had a bust of Plato, and John Adams read Plato, Aristotle, and others.
- **Epicurus specifically**: The claim that founding fathers were guided by Epicurus is **overstated**. Epicurus's philosophy (atomism, hedonism, materialism) was known to some founders (Jefferson called himself an "Epicurean" in one private letter to William Short, 1819), but this was idiosyncratic, not representative of the founding generation collectively. Most founders drew primarily on Locke, Montesquieu, and Cicero, not on Epicurus.
- **Masonic / fraternal context**: This is the speaker's ideological overlay; while many founders were Freemasons (Washington, Franklin), the claim that Masonic ritual derives specifically from Epicurean philosophy is not documented in mainstream historical scholarship.

### Sources
- [Jefferson on Epicurus — Monticello.org](https://www.monticello.org/site/research-and-collections/jefferson-and-epicurus)
- [Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy — Epicurus](https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/epicurus/)
- [Founders Online (NARA)](https://founders.archives.gov/) — primary source letters of founders

## Claim 7: Plato's *Republic* describes "the murder of the just man" which early Christians treated as preparation for their Messiah.

### What the video claims
At lines 543–552, the speaker says Plato's *Republic* (c. 375 BCE) describes "the murder of the just man," who embodies good, true, and beautiful, and that early Christians believed this prepared them for their anointed one.

### Verdict: **MIXED (text is accurate; interpretation is contested)**

### Reasoning
- **The passage is real**: *Republic* Book 2, 361e–362a (spoken by Glaucon, not Socrates or Plato directly) presents a thought experiment: imagine a perfectly just man with a reputation for injustice, suffering all manner of evil up to and including crucifixion (Greek: ἀνασχινδυλευθήσεται — literally "impaled on a stake"). The just man being tormented is the *worst-case scenario* used to strip extrinsic rewards from justice so that Socrates can argue justice is worth pursuing for its own sake.
- **It is not a prophecy**: Glaucon is steel-manning the *opponent's* position so Socrates can refute it. This is a philosophical device, not a forecast or divinely inspired message.
- **Early Christian reading**: Some early apologists (notably **Justin Martyr** in *First Apology*, and **Clement of Alexandria**) did draw typological parallels between Plato's "just man" and Christ. But this is an *interpretation applied to* Plato, not a prophecy *issued by* Plato. The speaker frequently states that early Christians "believed Plato prepared them," which is true of their subjective reception, but the objective claim that Plato intended a messianic prophecy is false.

### Sources
- [Plato, *Republic* 2.362a — Perseus](http://www.perseus.tufts.edu/hopper/text?doc=Perseus:text:1999.01.0168:book%3D2:section%3D362a) — primary Greek text
- [Antigone Journal: "Plato the Prophet?"](https://antigonejournal.com/2024/03/plato-prophet-crucifixion/) — scholarly discussion of how early Christians read this passage messianically and why that reading is problematic
- [Justin Martyr, *First Apology* ch. 46 (newadvent.org)](https://www.newadvent.org/fathers/0126.htm) — primary text of Justin drawing parallels between Plato and Christ

## Claim 8: "Christianity is founded upon the culture and philosophy of the Greeks and not the Hebrews."

### What the video claims
This is the central thesis of the episode (lines 167–176): the foundation of Christianity is Greek philosophy, not Hebrew prophetic tradition.

### Verdict: **SCHOLARLY DEBATE**

### Reasoning
This is a contested historiographical claim, not a simple true/false. The scholarly landscape includes three main views:

1. **Hellenization thesis** (strongly argued by the speaker): Early Gentile Christianity adapted its framework from Greek philosophy and Roman civic religion, especially after the apostles died and Jewish influence declined. Proponents: Adolf von Harnack (19th c.), some modern scholars of early Christianity.

2. **Diversity thesis**: Early Christianity was not one thing but many competing strands — Jewish-Christian, Pauline, Johannine, Apocalyptic, Gnostic. Some were heavily Hellenized; others (Ebionites, Essene-like movements) were not. Proponents: Bart Ehrman, James Dunn, Larry Hurtado.

3. **Hebraic continuity thesis**: Even through Greek linguistic categories, the theological content of the New Testament remains rooted in Second Temple Judaism. Proponents: N.T. Wright, Richard Bauckham, the so-called "New Perspective on Paul."

The speaker is taking sides in a long-running scholarly debate. He is not factually wrong in noting the deep influence of Greek philosophy (this is mainstream), but he is overstating it as *the exclusive* foundation when a robust Jewish-Christian stream (Ebionites, Quartodecimans, Syriac Christianity) persisted into the 4th–5th centuries.

### Sources
- [Bart Ehrman — *The New Testament: A Historical Introduction to the Early Christian Writings* (Oxford)](https://global.oup.com/academic/product/the-new-testament-9780190203825)
- [Larry Hurtado — *Lord Jesus Christ: Devotion to Jesus in Earliest Christianity* (Eerdmans)](https://www.eerdmans.com/Products/9780802831675/lord-jesus-christ.aspx)
- [N.T. Wright — *Paul and the Faithfulness of God* (Fortress Press)](https://fortresspress.com/product/paul-and-the-faithfulness-of-god/)
- [Adolf von Harnack — *The Mission and Expansion of Christianity in the First Three Centuries*](https://archive.org/details/missionexpansion01harnuoft) — classic formulation of the Hellenization thesis

## Claim 9: The U.S. founding fathers studied Greek philosophers like Epicurus, linking them to Masonry; fraternal societies come from Greek influence.

### What the video claims
At lines 382–400, the speaker ties the founding fathers' study of Greek philosophy to Masonic fraternal societies, implying a thematic continuity between Greek philosophy, U.S. democracy, and modern Christianity.

### Verdict: **MIXED**

### Reasoning
- **Greek influence on U.S. democracy and architecture**: TRUE. The founders widely admired Greek democracy (though they filtered it through Roman republicanism and Enlightenment liberalism). The neoclassical architecture of Washington D.C. (columns, pediments) is explicitly Greek/Roman revival.
- **Study of Plato and Aristotle**: TRUE in general. Founders read widely in classical literature.
- **Epicurus specifically**: PARTIALLY TRUE. Thomas Jefferson called himself an "Epicurean" in a private letter (1819), but this was eccentric, not representative of the founding generation. Most drew on Locke, Montesquieu, and Cicero.
- **Masonic societies derive from Greek philosophy**: UNVERIFIABLE / SPECULATIVE. Freemasonry's rituals draw from 17th–18th century European stonemasons' guilds, Rosicrucianism, and Enlightenment symbolism. Direct derivation from Greek philosophy is not supported by mainstream Masonic historiography.

### Sources
- [Thomas Jefferson letter to William Short, 31 Oct 1819 (Founders Online)](https://founders.archives.gov/documents/Jefferson/98-01-02-0579) — primary source for Jefferson's self-identification as Epicurean
- [National Park Service — Neoclassical Architecture in D.C.](https://www.nps.gov/articles/neoclassical-architecture.htm)
- [Freemasonry — Britannica](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Freemasonry) — overview of Masonic origins

## Claim 12: Plato's *Republic* was written c. 375 BCE.

### What the video claims
At line 544, the speaker says *Republic* was written in 375 BCE.

### Verdict: **MIXED (within scholarly range)**

### Reasoning
The traditional dating of Plato's *Republic* places it in the **late 380s to mid-370s BCE** (c. 388–375 BCE). 375 BCE sits at the late end of the range and is accepted by some scholars (e.g., stylometric analysis suggests it is one of Plato's middle-period works). Others place it slightly earlier (c. 380–385 BCE). The date is not precisely fixed but is not an unreasonable estimate.

### Sources
- [Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy — Plato](https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/plato/)
- [The Republic (Plato) — Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Republic_%28Plato%29) — contains scholarly datings
