# Testing Christianity — Part 9 Fact-Check

## Framing note

This document fact-checks only **verifiable historical, textual, and scholarly claims** made in Part 9. Theological/interpretive positions (Paul’s authority, covenant theology, Sabbath observance, sacred-name doctrine, etc.) are excluded from verdicts because they belong to interpretive debates rather than checkable facts.

---

### 1. Eusebius on 1 and 2 Peter (Book III, Ch. 3) — **Accurate**

**Claim:** Eusebius wrote that Peter’s first epistle was acknowledged as genuine and used by the ancient elders, whereas the second epistle was not canonical but was still used by some.

**Finding:** This matches Eusebius, *Ecclesiastical History* III.3 (cf. newadvent.org/fathers/250103.htm): “One epistle of Peter, that called the first, is acknowledged as genuine. And this the ancient elders used freely in their own writings as an undisputed work. But we have learned that his extant second Epistle does not belong to the canon; yet, as it has appeared profitable to many, it has been used with the other Scriptures.”

**Sources:**
- [Eusebius, *Church History* III.3 — New Advent](https://www.newadvent.org/fathers/250103.htm)

### 2. Eusebius on the Ebionites (Book III, Ch. 27) — **Substantially accurate, with paraphrase**

**Claim:** Eusebius describes two Ebionite factions: (a) those who regarded Jesus as merely a man born of normal intercourse, and (b) those who accepted the virgin birth and Holy Spirit but still rejected Paul as an apostate, kept the Sabbath, circumcision, and used only the Gospel according to the Hebrews.

**Finding:** Eusebius III.27 does describe two groups under the name “Ebionites”: one denying the virgin birth and another accepting it while still rejecting Paul’s epistles, observing Torah, and using the Hebrew Gospel. The speaker’s quotation is a loose paraphrase rather than a literal citation, but the substance is correct. The etymology of *Ebionim* = “poor” in Hebrew/Aramaic is correct; Eusebius explicitly says the name signified the “poverty of their understanding.”

**Sources:**
- [Eusebius, *Church History* III.27 — New Advent](https://www.newadvent.org/fathers/250103.htm)

---

### 3. Marcion accepted only Luke and Paul, and was the first to arrange a Christian canon — **Accurate**

**Claim:** Marcion accepted only the Gospel of Luke and the Pauline epistles (expurgated) and was the first to arrange a Christian canon.

**Finding:** This is standard early church history. Tertullian (*Against Marcion* IV), Irenaeus (*Against Heresies* I.27), and Epiphanius (*Panarion* 42) all attest that Marcion used a shortened Gospel (based on Luke) and ten Pauline letters. Modern scholarship generally agrees Marcion was the first to codify a Christian canon, even if his texts were redacted. The fact that orthodox response to Marcion spurred canon development is also widely accepted.

**Sources:**
- [Tertullian, *Against Marcion* — New Advent](https://www.newadvent.org/fathers/0312.htm)
- [Marcion of Sinope — Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Marcion_of_Sinope)

### 4. Encyclopaedia Britannica quotation about Marcion — **Accurate in substance, wording unverified**

**Claim:** “Marcion is perhaps best known for his treatment of Scripture. Though he rejected the Old Testament as the work of the Creator God, he did not deny its efficacy for those who do not believe in Christ. … He accepted as authentic all of the Pauline letters and the Gospel according to Luke after he had expurgated them of Judaizing elements.”

**Finding:** The speaker presents this as a Britannica quotation. The *substance* matches standard encyclopedia summaries of Marcion, but without an exact source URL or edition, the precise wording cannot be independently verified here. No error of substance.

### 5. Eusebius’ canon list (Book III, Ch. 25) — **Mostly accurate, condensed**

**Claim:** Eusebius classified New Testament writings into accepted, disputed, and rejected categories; Paul’s epistles were undisputed, while James, Jude, 2 Peter, 2–3 John were disputed, and Acts of Paul, Shepherd, Apocalypse of Peter, Barnabas, etc., were rejected.

**Finding:** The speaker is clearly summarizing Eusebius III.25. The gist is correct: the Holy Quaternion of the Gospels, Acts, Paul’s epistles, 1 John, 1 Peter are listed as accepted; James, Jude, 2 Peter, 2–3 John are disputed; Acts of Paul, Shepherd, Apocalypse of Peter, Barnabas, etc. are rejected. The speaker’s ordering and phrasing are slightly condensed, but no factual misrepresentation.

**Sources:**
- [Eusebius, *Church History* III.25 — New Advent](https://www.newadvent.org/fathers/250103.htm)

### 6. Origen: “Paul was, after Jesus, the founder of the churches that are in Christ” — **Cited widely, but exact wording uncertain from primary source in this session**

**Claim:** Origen wrote that Paul was, after Jesus, the founder of the churches that are in Christ.

**Finding:** The series frequently quotes from David Bercot’s *Dictionary of Early Christian Beliefs* rather than directly from critical editions. This quotation appears in that reference work and in other modern compilations of patristic testimony on Paul, but the exact form is not immediately traceable to a single standard Origen text in this session. It is consistent with Origen’s high view of Paul (e.g., *Commentary on Romans*, *Contra Celsum*). Verdict: **plausibly authentic compilation, but not independently verified here.**

---

### 7. Tertullian on Paul rebuking Peter and adding a new gospel form — **Accurate in substance**

**Claim:** Tertullian said that Paul “added yet another form of the gospel in addition to that which Peter and the rest had previously set forth.”

**Finding:** Tertullian, in *On the Prescription of Heretics* or *Against Marcion*, does argue that Marcionites use the incident of Paul rebuking Peter to claim Paul introduced a different gospel. The speaker’s wording is paraphrastic but captures Tertullian’s actual line of argument. The attribution is sound.

**Sources:**
- [Tertullian, *On the Prescription of Heretics* / *Against Marcion* — New Advent](https://www.newadvent.org/fathers/0312.htm)

---

### 8. Disputation of Archelaus and Manes: Paul as foundation of the Christian church — **Textually plausible; wording unverified here**

**Claim:** The *Disputation of Archelaus and Manes* says Paul’s writings are the foundation of the Christian church.

**Finding:** The *Acts of the Disputation of Archelaus with Manes* (4th c., anti-Manichaean) contains arguments about apostolic authority. The specific wording given by the speaker is not independently verified in this session. The attribution is possible, but the exact line should be treated as **unconfirmed** pending direct consultation of the text.

### 9. Biblical quotations (2 Peter 3:15–16; 1 Cor 7:18–19; Acts 18:12–13; 21:17–21, 27–28; Acts 15:19–21; Genesis 17:10–11; Mark 12:30) — **Accurate**

**Claim:** Multiple passages are quoted from 2 Peter, 1 Corinthians, Acts, Genesis, and Mark.

**Finding:** All quotations match standard English versions (NKJV/NASB-style). No textual errors.

---

### 10. “Paul was the founder of Christianity” / “No Christianity without Paul” — **Interpretive claim, not adjudicated**

**Claim:** “There would be no Christianity without Paul,” and “Paul became the influence of Christianity.”

**Finding:** This is a historical-theological judgment, not a checkable fact in the same way as a date or a citation. Many scholars (e.g., Bart Ehrman, Paula Fredriksen, N.T. Wright) debate the extent of Paul’s role in shaping what became orthodox Christianity. The speaker’s framing is a defensible historical argument (Paul’s letters dominate the NT and patristic debate) but it is an **interpretation**, not a falsifiable datum.

---

### 11. Acts 15: the Jerusalem agreement expected Gentiles to learn Torah in synagogues — **Interpretive inference**

**Claim:** The Jerusalem Council (Acts 15:19–21) expected Gentile converts to abstain from idols, sexual immorality, strangled things, and blood, and then to learn the rest in synagogues every Sabbath where Moses was read.

**Finding:** The *quotation* is accurate. The *inference* that the apostles intended Gentiles to keep learning Torah in synagogues is a plausible reading but remains an **interpretation** of the passage’s purpose. Mainstream biblical scholarship offers multiple readings of the “Apostolic Decree.”
