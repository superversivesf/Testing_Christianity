# Testing Christianity — Part 9 Summary
## How the New Testament Became Centered Around Paul

**Source:** YouTube transcript (`09 - TESTING_CHRISTIANITY_PT._9_HOW_THE_NEW_TESTAMENT_BECAME_CENTERED_AROUND_PAUL.txt`)
**Length:** ~35 minutes

---

### Opening & Recap (00:00–01:00)

- Speaker reminds viewers that the series is topical and should be watched in order.
- Recaps Part 8: reviewed early church fathers and their foundational beliefs.
- Observes that many listeners distance themselves from the fathers once they learn what those men actually taught.
- Argues that "cherry-picking" is not the issue; the problem is replacement theology and covenant hijacking, which the speaker claims are proven by the fathers’ own writings.
- Christianity did not appear in a vacuum between Acts and the present day; doctrines were built incrementally over ~300 years (1st–4th centuries).
- The fathers are “tares” (citing Matt 13) because their foundation was false, even though some of their beliefs were correct.

### The Fate of 2 Peter (01:00–02:50)

- Reads Eusebius, *Church History* Book III, Section 3: 1 Peter was accepted as genuine by early elders; 2 Peter was “uncanonical” and disputed, though some valued it.
- Speaker suggests the church fathers resisted 2 Peter because it warned against destructive doctrines and false teachers (2 Pet 2–3) and told believers to wait for Yah’s promise—while the fathers claimed the promise no longer applied.
- Conclusion: the fathers accepted Hebrew writings they agreed with and rejected those they did not.

### Jewish Believers Rejected by the Church: The Ebionites (02:50–05:40)

- Acknowledges there were true Jewish believers in Messiah, but the winning faction (Christianity) marginalized them.
- Reads Eusebius again (*Church History* Book III, Chapter on the Ebionites) describing two Ebionite factions:
  1. Those who denied the virgin birth and regarded Jesus as merely a virtuous man.
  2. Those who accepted the virgin birth and Holy Spirit but still rejected Paul as an apostate from the law and used only the Gospel according to the Hebrews.
- The latter group kept the Sabbath, circumcision, and Torah (except the sacrificial atonement laws, believing Hebrews teaches Jesus replaced them).
- Speaker points out the insult: “Ebionites” means “poor” in Hebrew; Eusebius weaponized the word to imply “poor in understanding.”
- These Torah-keeping Jewish believers were labeled heretics by the patristic church.

### The Conflict with Paul (05:40–09:00)

- Speaker: many in modern Hebrew-Israelite circles blame Paul for starting Christianity.
- Personally, the speaker does not reject Paul, because he trusts the scriptures that have been preserved—including Paul’s letters.
- Nevertheless, Paul “brings all the confusion”; without his letters, many lawless excuses would not exist.
- Enter Marcion:
  - Reads from a reference source (*Dictionary of Early Christian Beliefs* / Britannica) describing Marcion as a leading 2nd-century heretical teacher with Gnostic elements.
  - Marcion accepted only the Gospel of Luke and the Pauline epistles (expurgated of “Judaizing” elements) for his canon.
  - He was the first to arrange a Christian canon and, because of him, the orthodox church was forced to define what was canon.
  - Marcion’s theology: the OT Creator God is different from Jesus’ Father; the OT God was a lower, law-giving deity, not omnipotent.
  - Speaker: Marcion severed the Old Testament from Christianity, yet he is the reason the church centered on Paul.
  - Debates with Marcion (Irenaeus, Tertullian, Origen) led to heavy reliance on Paul, making Paul the foundation of Christian doctrine.

### The Speaker’s Personal Stance on Paul (09:00–18:00)

- The speaker does not believe Paul created Christianity; rather, Paul’s writings were *used* to justify Christian doctrines.
- Cites 2 Peter 3:15–16: Paul wrote things hard to understand, which untaught and unstable people twist to their own destruction.
- Speaker says he would have been more with Peter than with Paul (acknowledging they had a falling-out).
- Paul’s letters are a “test for Gentiles”: if they truly love Yah, they will read the *whole* Bible, not just Paul.
- People who read Paul first are “untaught” in Peter’s sense; Christianity is full of unlearned people making doctrines of destruction.
- Recommends reading Torah and the full scriptures before reading Paul; Paul’s epistles are situational letters, not a new Torah.

### Acts Evidence of Jewish Opposition to Paul (18:00–20:00)

- Cites Acts 18:12–13: Jews accused Paul before Gallio of persuading men to worship God contrary to the law.
- Cites Acts 21:17–21: Jewish believers who were zealous for the law reported that Paul taught Jews among the Gentiles to forsake Moses and not circumcise their children.
- Cites Acts 21:27–28: Asian Jews seized Paul in the temple, accusing him of teaching against the people, the law, and the place, and of bringing Greeks into the temple.
- Speaker argues these passages explain why Torah-keeping Jewish believers (Ebionites) were against Paul.

### Circumcision (20:00–24:00)

- The speaker affirms circumcision for natural branches of Israel, citing Genesis 17:10–11 as an everlasting covenant sign.
- The Acts 15 discussion, he argues, concerned Gentiles, not the natural branches.
- Shares personal testimony about his struggle to get his youngest sons circumcised and his belief that the custom has never gone away.

### Patristic Quotes Elevating Paul (24:00–29:00)

- Quotes Origen: “Paul was, after Jesus, the founder of the churches that are in Christ.”
- Quotes Tertullian (paraphrasing the argument of Peter being rebuked by Paul): Paul “added yet another form of the gospel in addition to that which Peter and the rest had previously set forth.”
- Quotes the *Disputation of Archelaus and Manes*: Paul’s writings are the foundation of the Christian church.
- Speaker: these prove Peter’s warning came true—untaught men twisted Paul into a new gospel.

### Eusebius’ Canon List (29:00–32:00)

- Reads Eusebius, *Church History* Book III, Ch. 25: summarizes the categories of New Testament writings:
  - **Accepted**: Four Gospels, Acts, Paul’s epistles, 1 John, 1 Peter; “if it seems proper,” Revelation of John.
  - **Disputed but recognized by many**: James, Jude, 2 Peter, 2–3 John.
  - **Rejected**: Acts of Paul, Shepherd, Apocalypse of Peter, Epistle of Barnabas, Teachings of the Apostles.
  - “If it seems proper” for Revelation; some reject it, others class it with accepted books.
  - Some also place the Gospel according to the Hebrews among disputed books.
- Observation: these men chose what is in the New Testament. Paul’s epistles were undisputed; other Jewish-leaning books were disputed or rejected.

### Conclusion (32:00–35:00)

- Paul is not the author of our faith; Yahuwah is.
- Anyone teaching lawlessness should be rejected, but the speaker does not believe Paul taught lawlessness—only that he is misunderstood.
- Without Paul’s letters there would be far less confusion, but the problem is the readers, not Paul.
- Most Christians operate from the “spirit of Marcion”: they only understand the Father through the New Testament (mostly Paul) and ignore the Old Testament.
- Next episode will finally discuss Constantine.
- Christianity is a surface-level biblical religion that, at its doctrinal core, abuses grace, edits covenants, and rejects the Father’s chosen way.
- Call to action: viewers should leave the “religion of tares” and come to the Father in truth, then proceed to Part 10.
