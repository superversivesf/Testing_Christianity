# Episode 3 — Summary: The Religion of Christianity Is the Tares That Satan Planted

**Source:** `03 - TESTING_CHRISTIANITY_PT._3_THE_RELIGION_OF_CHRISTIANITY_IS_THE_TARES_THAT_SATAN_PLANTED.txt`

**Overall thesis:** The speaker lays out the central claim of the series: Christianity, as a global religion, is the fulfillment of Jesus' Parable of the Wheat and Tares (Matthew 13:24-43). It is a counterfeit belief system planted by Satan that looks identical to true faith while growing, but reveals itself as false at the harvest. The speaker argues that the sheer size, doctrinal divisions, and worldly compromise of Christianity are evidence that it is the "broad gate" leading to destruction, not the narrow way of which Jesus spoke.

## 4. The Core Prophecy: The Parable of the Wheat and the Tares (lines 114-198)

- **Matthew 13:24-30 quoted (lines 117-136):**
  - Kingdom of heaven is like a man who sowed good seed in his field.
  - While men slept, his enemy sowed tares among the wheat.
  - The servants asked: "Sir, did you not sow good seed in your field? How then does it have tares?"
  - The owner answered: "An enemy has done this."
  - The servants asked if they should gather the tares up.
  - He said: "No, lest while you gather up the tares, you also uproot the wheat with them. Let both grow together until the harvest... First gather together the tares and bind them in bundles to burn them, but gather the wheat into my barn."
- **Matthew 13:36-43 quoted (the explanation, lines 138-158):**
  - He who sows the good seed is the Son of Man.
  - The field is the world.
  - The good seeds are the sons of the kingdom.
  - The tares are the sons of the wicked one.
  - The enemy who sowed them is the devil.
  - The harvest is the end of the age; the reapers are the angels.
  - At the end of the age, the Son of Man will send angels to gather out of his kingdom all things that offend and those who practice lawlessness, casting them into the furnace of fire.
  - Then the righteous will shine forth as the sun in the kingdom of their Father.
- **Botanical explanation of tares:**
  - Tares are "indistinguishable from wheat until the final fruit appears" (lines 162-163).
  - This is why the enemy used tares specifically — counterfeit believers who look and act like true believers but do not produce righteous fruit in the end (lines 181-182, 321-322).
- **Application to the present:**
  - "We are in the harvest right now" (line 160).
  - The righteous (wheat) and the counterfeit (tares) are growing together in the same field (the world) (lines 179, 314-316).
  - This is **not** about overt Satanists and devil worshippers — "they don't look like us. The tares are the counterfeit who look and act like believers" (lines 319-321).
  - Many with doctrines, false traditions, and believers who say they love him but "side with the world more than they ever care about him" are counterfeit (lines 183-185).
  - They practice traditions of men before turning to the word of Elohim (line 186-187).
  - They deeply offend those preaching righteousness and living according to the word (lines 188-189).

**Key theological terms used in this episode:**
- **Yahushua** — the speaker's preferred Hebrew-name rendering of Jesus.
- **Yahuwah** — the speaker's preferred rendering of the divine name (YHWH).
- **Elohim** — used as a title for God/the Father.
- **Adonai** — used as "my master/Lord" when quoting Matthew 7.
- **Yashorel** — the speaker's term for biblical Israel, with which he identifies the African diaspora.
- **Tares** — *Lolium temulentum* (darnel), a weed resembling wheat until maturity, used in Matthew 13.
- **Replacement theology / supersessionism** — the Christian doctrine that the Church has replaced Israel as the people of God, which the speaker rejects.
- **Ineffable name doctrine** — the Jewish (and later Christian) practice of avoiding pronunciation of the divine name, substituting titles like "Lord" or "God."
- **Hellenization** — the spread of Greek language, culture, and philosophical categories, framed by the speaker as Satan's strategy.
- **Social gospel** — using Christian faith toward political/social improvement rather than spiritual salvation, which the speaker rejects.
