# Episode 13 — Alexander the Great and the Quest to Make the Whole World Greek

## Overview

Part 13 transitions from the Greek philosophers (Part 12) to the military-political vehicle that spread Greek culture across the known world: **Hellenization**. The speaker argues that Alexander the Great and his successors did not merely conquer territory but exported a comprehensive Greek identity—language, religion, architecture, athletics, and philosophy—to non-Greek populations. This process, the speaker claims, is the hidden mechanism by which Satan sought to reverse the division of languages at Babel and unite humanity under a single counter-God culture. The episode frames Christianity itself as the modern continuation of this Hellenizing project.

## Detailed Summary

### Introduction and Recap (00:00–01:15)
- The speaker opens with the standard series reminder that these videos are sequential and should be watched from Part 1.
- Recaps Part 12: Greek philosophers (Socrates, Plato, Aristotle) are presented as "prophets of the Greeks" used by Satan to prepare the Roman world for a new religion.
- Christianity is framed as a "religion of tares" planted by Satan, correlating Greek philosophical foundations with the Hebrew faith.
- The episode will fill a "missing link": understanding **Hellenization**—how Greek culture spread into the Roman Empire and how the set-apart culture of Yashareel (Israel), specifically the Yahudim (Jews) in Judea, interacted with it.

### Correspondence and the Question of Assimilation (01:16–02:20)
- The speaker reads from a correspondent who argued:
  - Greek was the *lingua franca* of the Roman Empire, and most Jews spoke Greek.
  - There is no Hebrew or Aramaic Bible prior to the early 4th century.
  - Speaking Greek does not make one Greek (analogous to Spanish-speaking people not being Spanish).
- The speaker acknowledges some truth in the correspondent's claims (many Jews did speak Greek) but insists that the **Yahudim in Judea** resisted assimilation.
- He cites **Josephus** as evidence that the Yahudim "did not have a culture of learning the other languages of the world" and frowned upon it.

### Why Hebrew Is "Special" (02:21–04:05)
- A second correspondent asked: "Why do you think Hebrew is some special language?"
- The speaker rejects the idea that God has "different names at different times," calling it a doctrine that leads to Satan.
- Hebrew is special because:
  - It is the language of the people God chose and dwelled among.
  - It is the only language in which the commandments were written.
  - It is the "language of creation," per the **Book of Jubilees** 12:25–28 (cited in full), where God speaks to Abraham in Hebrew, "the tongue of creation."
- He notes that books like Jubilees were excluded from the Bible because they reveal this truth.

### Defining Hellenization (04:06–05:24)
- The speaker defines **Hellenization**: "the adoption of Greek culture, religion, language, and identity by non-Greeks."
- He reads from **Encyclopedia Britannica** on the **Hellenistic Age**:
  - Period: death of Alexander the Great (323 BCE) to Roman conquest of Egypt (30 BCE).
  - Extended "for some purposes" to Constantine's move of the capital to Constantinople (330 CE)—spanning almost 600 years.
  - Numerous realms (Macedonian, Seleucid, Ptolemaic) served as the framework for spreading Greek-Hellenic culture and the fusion of Greek and Eastern elements.
- Speaker summarizes: the promotion of Greek culture, language, and lifestyle lasted from 323 BCE to 330 CE—nearly 600 years.

### Alexander the Great: Conquest and Cultural Export (05:25–09:48)
- Background: In the second half of the 4th century BCE, the **Macedonians** (Greeks of northern Greece) unified the Greek peninsula, even though Athens and Sparta had been divided by the Peloponnesian War.
- **King Philip II** was murdered in 336 BCE; his 20-year-old son **Alexander** succeeded him, despite suspicion of involvement in his father's death.
- Many Greek city-states showed little enthusiasm for Alexander's rule; animosity between Macedonians and Greeks was so intense that some Greeks refused to serve in his army.
- Alexander was a student of **Aristotle** and displayed remarkable military skills.
- His army conquered **Asia Minor, Egypt, Syria, Babylon, and Persia**.
- Alexander began adopting Persian ways, including demanding to be worshipped as divine, which repulsed many soldiers.
- **Died in 323 BCE at age 33** from a fever.
- He left no central organization for the conquered territories; he mostly placed Macedonians in charge of existing governments.
- However, he and his successors constructed or modified cities to conform to a **Greek model**:
  - Greek architecture, religion, athletics, and culture were introduced.
  - Greek cities sprang up along the Mediterranean coast of Judea and Samaria.
  - **Alexandria** in Egypt became the home of the ancient world's greatest library (later burned down by the Romans).
- **Egypt** welcomed Alexander as a liberator from Persian rule, placed him on the throne of the pharaohs, gave him the crowns of Upper and Lower Egypt, and named him the incarnation of **Ra and Osiris**.
- He set in motion plans for Alexandria, the second-largest city in Egypt, named after him.

### The Analogy of American Influence (09:49–10:22)
- The speaker compares Alexander's method to a hypothetical American conquest of Iraq:
  - America leaves the Iraqi government intact but imports shopping malls, movies, churches, and the American way of life.
  - The conquered people remain in place but start living like the conquerors.
- This, he argues, is how Hellenism works: bring in the culture and make people Greeks without replacing the local government.

### The Diadochi: Seleucids and Ptolemies (10:23–12:28)
- After Alexander's death (323 BCE), his empire was divided among his generals.
- **Seleucus** took control of much of the old Persian Empire—**Asia Minor, modern Turkey, and Syria**.
- **Ptolemy** governed Egypt; he allegedly **hijacked Alexander's corpse** and used it as proof of his succession.
- They established the **Seleucid** and **Ptolemaic dynasties**.
- The spread of Greek culture persisted throughout the period of warfare between these competing dynasties.
- Macedonian rulers promoted the Greek language and customs, exposing diverse cultures to the philosophies of **Socrates, Plato, and Aristotle**.
- **Athenian democracy** was not spread to these lands because it would undermine the rulers' power.
- For nearly 300 years, warfare continued between the Seleucid and Ptolemaic dynasties before the Romans eventually took over.

### Romans as Continuators of Greek Culture (12:29–14:08)
- The speaker asserts: "The Roman culture is not Roman culture. It is a continuance of Greek culture, just with the Romans in control of it."
- Rome swept over the Greeks in the 2nd century BCE, plundering Greek art and sculpture.
- Rome conquered the Greeks but took their culture as their own, continuing Hellenization.
- Roman architecture, art, literature, and religion all showed Greek influence—under the banner of Rome.
- Upper-class Roman families imported Greek tutors and sent young men to Athens to study philosophy, arts, and science.

### Genesis 11 and the Satanic Goal of Cultural Unity (14:09–16:16)
- The speaker returns to **Genesis 11:4–8** (the Tower of Babel), reading the full passage.
- Satan's goal was to unite humanity under one tongue to rebel against God collectively.
- After God confused the languages, Satan did not abandon this goal.
- The speaker draws a parallel to **English** today as the modern global language, functioning as a new Babel-like unifying tongue.
- After establishing Greek philosophers as "prophets," Satan set in motion the events to spread Greek culture.
- The Romans, wicked in themselves, didn't change Greek culture; they said they would do a better job of spreading it.

### Christianity and Modern Hellenization (16:17–18:37)
- The speaker claims that **Hellenization has never stopped** and that **Christianity is its main modern vehicle**.
- Christians are described as "Hellenized disciples spreading the culture of the Greeks in the guise of their religion."
- Examples of Hellenized Christian practice:
  - Using the name **Jesus** instead of a Hebrew name.
  - Protecting Greek language and culture at all costs.
  - Rejecting the set-apart Hebrew way.
- The basis for Greek association with Israel is the claim that the **New Testament was written in Greek**.
- The speaker counters that the early church fathers (e.g., Irenaeus, Clement of Alexandria) say **Matthew was originally written in Hebrew**.
- The truth, he argues, is not that the NT was written in Greek, but that the only surviving manuscripts are Greek.
- He frames his own ministry as a rejection of Hellenization and calls on viewers to reject it too.

### Greek Influence in the Modern World (18:38–22:38)
- The speaker lists pervasive Greek cultural influences today:
  - **Architecture**: U.S. Capitol and many public buildings use classical Greek columns and pediments, not Roman.
    - This is described as an embrace of "mystery religions and satanic goals hidden within secret societies."
  - **Sports**: Basketball, football, and soccer competitions derive from Greek culture; the origin of soccer can be traced to ancient Greece.
  - **Theater**: Greek theater was a major part of Greek life and persists today.
  - **Medicine and education**: Influenced by the Greeks.
  - **Graduation ceremonies**: Formal ceremonies with diplomas draw from Greek education. The word **"diploma"** comes from Greek for "double-folded paper."
  - **Democracy**: The whole government system comes from the Greeks and is spread worldwide.
  - He plays a clip of **Barack Obama** speaking about elections and democracy in Tunisia, Egypt, Libya, Malawi, Senegal, Somalia, and Burma as evidence of the global reach of this Greek-derived system.
- The speaker clarifies he is not saying playing sports makes one evil; he wants viewers to understand where modern culture comes from.

### The Spread of Greek Philosophy and the Hebrew Mindset (22:39–24:39)
- The speaker emphasizes the influence of **Greek philosophy** on how people think.
- Hebrews were taught to **apply** what God told them; Greeks used **reason and logic** ("if it says this, then it means this").
- This "Greek reasoning" is the source of most major false doctrines in Christianity.
- Understanding this difference is key to understanding why confusion exists in Christian theology.

### Yahudim and the Diaspora in Egypt (24:40–29:08)
- There was only one nation that truly resisted Hellenization: the **Yahudim in Judea**.
- **Ptolemy** took over Egypt and, shortly after (320 BCE), went to Judea. The Yahudim made no effort to defend themselves because it was the **Sabbath**.
  - The Greeks found this ridiculous and viewed the Yahudim as foolish.
- According to **Josephus**, Ptolemy showed kindness to the Yahudim.
- According to **Hecataeus of Abdera** (cited by Josephus in *Against Apion*), many Jews moved to Egypt because of this kindness.
- In **311 BCE**, the high priest **Hezekiah** went with Ptolemy to Egypt.
- Jewish settlements grew along the Mediterranean coast, Greek islands, and Black Sea shores—this is the beginning of the **Jewish diaspora**.
- Because these Jews were separated from their homeland, they began to adapt to Hellenization:
  - Learned Greek and adopted some customs.
  - Alexandria became a major center.

### The Septuagint and Cultural Assimilation (29:09–31:40)
- To be a citizen in a Greek city involved participation in the city's religion (patron gods, festivals, athletic games bound up with pagan sacrifices).
  - Jews could not participate, so they excluded themselves from full citizenship but were not treated as foreigners.
- The Ptolemies permitted Jews to organize **separate communities** within Alexandria with their own courts, Torah law, and synagogues.
- Over generations, assimilation deepened:
  - First generation: little Greek.
  - Second generation: bilingual (Hebrew and Greek).
  - Third generation: little Hebrew; native tongue was Greek.
- They could not read Hebrew, creating a spiritual problem for those who did not want to abandon their faith entirely.
- Parents encouraged children to study Hebrew, but they also **translated the Torah into Greek**.
- The speaker states: "This is called the Pentateuch, simply meaning the Pentateuch, which means the 70, and because 70 learned scribes were a part of this translation, the Pentateuch was called the Septuagint, which means the 70."
- These assimilated Yahudim began to merge with the Greeks, creating confusion and trouble.

### The Surrounding World Turns Greek (31:41–33:01)
- On every side of Judea, old cities (Ammonite, Syrian, Philistine) assumed new Greek names and practiced the Greek way of life.
- Even old gods were given Greek names.
- The Yahudim were surrounded by a changing world becoming Greek.
- Those who embraced Greek culture and trade prospered; those who resisted (the Yahudim in Judea) were left behind, leading many to scatter around Greece and Asia Minor.

### Paul and the Need for Historical Context (33:02–34:21)
- Because this history is not known, people misunderstand when Paul speaks about "the Greeks" in the New Testament.
- Many believe Paul is speaking about the Greek philosophers' society, missing the history of Jewish rejection of Hellenization.
- The speaker will continue covering this topic in the next video to remove confusion.

## Key Themes

1. **Hellenization as a Satanic strategy**: The unifying of humanity under one culture (reversing Babel) to rebel against God.
2. **Alexander as a cultural exporter, not just a conqueror**: His true innovation was importing Greek city life, architecture, athletics, and philosophy rather than imposing direct rule.
3. **Romans as Greek culture's successor**: Rome didn't replace Greek culture; it adopted and spread it further under its own banner.
4. **Jewish resistance vs. diaspora assimilation**: The Yahudim in Judea resisted, but diaspora Jews in Alexandria gradually assimilated over three generations.
5. **Christianity as modern Hellenization**: Using Greek names (Jesus), Greek philosophy (logic/reasoning over Hebrew application), and Greek institutional frameworks (Sunday, architecture).
6. **Hebrew as the only "set-apart" language**: Citing Jubilees, the speaker argues Hebrew is the language of creation and covenant, making resistance to Greek cultural adoption a divine command.
