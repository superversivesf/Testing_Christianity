## Setting the Stage: The World at the End of Acts (~01:30 – ~03:00)

- **The end of the Book of Acts:** Paul has made it to Rome to plead his case to Caesar.
- **Year:** around **62 AD** under **Emperor Nero**.
- **Political context:** The Roman Empire is the major world power, controlling much of the known world.
- The **Yahudim** (Jews) are still a nation in the land called **Judea** (the speaker notes some people call it Palestine today, but it was not called Palestine at that time).
  - The speaker acknowledges he is using conventional maps and narratives for the sake of the presentation.
- **Jerusalem** and the **Temple** still stand.
  - Many Jews who rejected Yahusha as Messiah still used the Temple for animal sacrifices for atonement.
- **Jewish diaspora:** Many Yahudim were scattered throughout the Roman Empire, especially in **Asia Minor**.
  - These diaspora Jews had assimilated with Greek culture during the period of **Hellenization**.
  - They are referred to as the "dispersion among the Greeks" (cited from John 7:33–36).
  - The speaker argues these assimilated Jews were often called "Greeks" because they had "sold out."
  - This is why Paul went to **synagogues**, not pagan temples, when spreading the message of Messiah.

## The Growth of the Movement and the Label "Christian" (~03:00 – ~04:12)

- Because of Paul and the other apostles, belief in Yahusha was spreading throughout the Roman world.
- Many assemblies were being set up due to Paul’s missionary journeys.
- The new believers were **not initially persecuted by Rome**; rather, the **Yahudim** (unbelieving Jews) dealt more harshly with them than Rome did.
- Over time, belief in Yahusha (a man who rose from the dead) began to be accepted by Gentiles.
- The label **"Christian"** began to be used.
  - It was **not accepted by the disciples and apostles** at this time.
  - It was a **derogatory term** used by outsiders mocking the new faith.
  - The apostles called themselves **disciples** and said they followed **"the Way."**
  - The speaker compares the evolution of "Christian" from derogatory to accepted label to other historical slurs-turned-labels.

## Persecution of the Apostles and the Later New Testament Letters (~04:12 – ~05:50)

- As time went on and the movement grew, the apostles began to be persecuted.
- **Paul:**
  - According to his other letters, he was arrested again.
  - At a preliminary hearing, he realized he would not be released and would face death.
  - Only **Luke** was with him; he was lonely and wanted to see **Timothy** one more time.
  - He wrote his **final letter to Timothy** (2 Timothy), asking him to come at once.
  - He expressed his final thoughts in the letter in case Timothy did not arrive in time.
  - We do not know whether Timothy made it (cited: 2 Timothy 4).
  - The summary of **2 Timothy**: leaders of the faith are to be unashamed of the gospel and carry on faithfully with the message about Messiah.
- **Peter:**
  - The **first and second epistles of Peter** are said to have been written during **Nero’s persecution**.
  - **1 Peter:** written to believers in Asia Minor to encourage them, explain suffering, and remind them of eternal reward.
  - **2 Peter:** written before his expected death to combat false doctrine spreading throughout Rome.
    - Peter confronted falsehoods so they would not be mistaken for the truth.
    - He reminded them not to give up expecting the return of Yahusha.
- **Epistle of Jude:** very similar to 2 Peter.
- All four of these letters (2 Timothy, 1 Peter, 2 Peter, Jude) show a separation from the Yahudim and primarily deal with the believers/assemblies.
- These believers were **labeled as Christians by Nero**.
- It was **not the Yahudim in general** who were being persecuted, but the **Yahudim who believed in Messiah**.
  - First persecuted by their own people, then by Rome, because they were labeled as troublemakers bringing Gentiles into a strange religion.

## The Jewish War (66–70/73 AD) (~05:50 – ~07:00)

- **The event that finalized the separation:** the **Yahudim war** that resulted in the destruction of the Temple, the end of animal sacrifices, and the scattering of the Yahudim.
- **Yahusha’s prophecy cited:** Matthew 24:1–2 — "not one stone shall be left here upon another that shall not be thrown down."
  - The fulfillment is not described in the New Testament, but history records it.
- **Source:** **Flavius Josephus** (Yosef ben Matayahu), a Jewish Roman citizen and historian.
  - Wrote *Antiquities of the Jews* and *The Jewish War*.
  - Said to have originally written *The Jewish War* in Hebrew so diaspora Jews could read it, later translating it into Greek with help.
  - In *The Jewish War*, he shows how Yahusha’s prophecy came true: **not one stone of the building was left standing** after the war of 70 AD.
- **Timeline of the war:**
  - The events of **66 to 73 AD** brought untold suffering.
  - Roman governor appointed over Judea in **64 AD**: **Gessius Florus**, described as corrupt and cruel.
  - After raiding the treasury of the Temple, Jewish resentment boiled over.
  - The Pharisees joined forces with the rejuvenated **Zealot movement** in organizing a revolt.
  - **Herod Agrippa II** (the Edomite ruler; the one Paul appeared before in Acts 26) urged the Jews to be at peace but was rejected; he stayed loyal to Rome.
  - Rebel leader **Menahem** led men to capture the Roman fortress at **Masada**, then captured the Roman garrison in Jerusalem.
  - Menahem was killed in **66 AD**.
  - Early rebel success caused revolution fever.
  - They expected a complete victory as in the days of the **Maccabean Revolt** over two centuries earlier.
  - Victory seemed guaranteed in **November 66 AD** when rebels defeated a Roman legion sent from Syria.
  - But Rome proved more powerful.
  - **Vespasian** arrived in Galilee in **early 67 AD**.
  - Vespasian’s forces consolidated gains and approached Jerusalem.
  - The war was briefly interrupted by **Nero’s apparent suicide** (Nero sentenced to death, killed himself).
  - Civil unrest in Rome after his death.
  - Vespasian resumed his offensive in **June 69 AD** and took all of Judea except Jerusalem.
  - He was proclaimed emperor and left Judea to take control of the Roman Empire.
  - This period is known as the **Year of the Four Emperors**.
  - In the **spring of 70 AD**, Vespasian sent his son **Titus** to crush the Jews.
  - Titus laid siege to Jerusalem in **April**.
  - The Jews endured five months.
  - The **last animal sacrifice** was offered on the great altar on **August 6**.
  - The **Temple was burned on August 28, 70 AD**.
  - The destruction of the entire city soon followed (by the end of September).
  - After the fire cooled, Roman troops reportedly pried apart the stonework to retrieve the gold that had melted between the cracks — a fulfillment of Yahusha’s prophecy.
  - The Romans subdued the three remaining rebel fortresses; the last was **Masada**, which fell in **May 73 AD** after its defenders committed mass suicide.
  - The Romans ruled Judea with an iron fist, treating it as occupied, hostile territory.

## Aftermath of the Temple’s Destruction (~07:00 – ~07:30)

- After the loss of the Temple, everything changed for the Yahudim.
- They had no way to properly worship Yahuwah.
- The **Sadducees** and the **priesthood** disappeared as factors in society.
- The **Pharisees** dominated; a new **Sanhedrin** (assembly of appointed rabbis) was established.
  - It mainly functioned as an institution to study the scriptures.
  - Synagogues continued as the institutions sustaining the religion of the Yahudim.
- There was **no tolerance of the movement of believers in Messiah** any longer.
- The Yahudim were without a Temple; by rejecting Yahusha, they had become a people without his heart and soul.
- Many Yahudim fled south, away from the Roman Empire.
- The speaker clarifies: this history is **not in reference to the current occupants of the land of Israel today**.
- This is all a confirmation of the curses found in **Deuteronomy 28**.

## The Second Jewish Revolt (Bar Kokhba, 132 AD) (~07:30 – ~08:30)

- After the first revolt, the Hebrews were no longer allowed by Rome to be their own sect with self-governance.
  - Yahusha had been able to minister precisely because Rome let the Jews govern their own people.
  - After 70 AD, that changed; consistent fighting and rebellion continued.
  - Viewers can research the Jewish wars from 66 AD to 136 AD.
- **Second revolt:** in **132 AD**, led by **Simon Bar Kokhba**.
  - **Tinius Rufus** was the Roman governor.
  - Jerusalem was still in ruins.
  - **Emperor Hadrian** wanted to rebuild Jerusalem as a Roman colony and assimilate it with pagan culture.
  - He dealt harshly with remaining Hebrews, placing restrictions on their way of life and **banning circumcisions**.
  - This led to a revolt led by Bar Kokhba; they were crushed by the Romans.
  - Hadrian **wiped the province of Judea off the Roman map**, renaming it **Syria-Palestina**.
  - He renamed Jerusalem **Aelia Capitolina** after himself and rebuilt it as a typical Roman town.
  - This is why the land is known as Palestine today (the speaker says it stayed this way until the 7th century Arab conquest).
  - Hebrews were not allowed in the city except for **one day a year at Tisha B'Av** (regarded as the saddest day in the Jewish calendar).
- The speaker frames this history as the consequence for those who "persecuted and crucified the Messiah sent to them."

  - What matters is **who took that label and carried it forward** into what we know as Christianity today.
