# Testing Christianity — Episode 7 Summary
## "The Unspoken History About the Birth of Christianity"

**Speaker:** "Truth Unedited" (sacred-name / Hebrew-Israelite perspective)
**Approx. runtime:** 35 minutes
**Transcript file:** `07 - TESTING_CHRISTIANITY_PT._7_THE_UNSPOKEN_HISTORY_ABOUT_THE_BIRTH_OF_CHRISTIANITY.txt`

## 1. Introduction: Bridging the Gap Between the Apostles and Constantine (lines 1–13)

- The speaker frames Part 7 as filling the historical gap between the apostolic era (Part 6) and the Constantinian era (later parts).
- He states that most people skip from the apostles being killed directly to Constantine and the Council of Nicaea (c. 325 CE), missing the crucial period when the theology of Roman Christianity was actually formed.
- The thesis: if you understand what happened in the first and second centuries after the apostles died, the standard apologetics for early Christianity ("the early church fathers prove the faith was true") collapse.
- He declares this episode will explain the actual moment Christianity was born.

## 2. The "Label" Problem: Christians, Yahudim, and the Crowd (lines 14–28)

- The speaker acknowledges that Jewish followers of Yahushua (his preferred Hebrew name for Jesus) also got called "Christians," but only because the crowd who rejected them used the label.
- He draws an analogy: just as people wrongly tie him to the Hebrew Israelite "camps" (extremist street-preachers) despite doctrinal differences, labels are imprecise.
- He sets aside the Jewish Yahushua-believers at this point, arguing that once Christianity became an institution, their influence vanished.
- The main object of study is therefore the *Gentile* church, because "the main church of Christianity is built on the back of Gentiles."

## 3. The Cult of Serapis and Pre-Existing "Christianos" (lines 29–149)

- Before belief in Jesus arrived in Rome, there was already a pagan cult called "Christianos."
- The cult worshipped Serapis, a Greco-Egyptian god.
- The speaker reads extensively from the World History Encyclopedia:
  - Serapis was created by Ptolemy I Soter (r. 305–282 BCE) as a syncretic deity blending Osiris, Apis, and Zeus to unite Greek and Egyptian subjects.
  - The cult spread throughout the Roman Empire and was among the most popular in Rome by the 1st century CE — the same era the gospel was spreading.
  - Serapis was known as a "redeemer god and savior" who granted eternal life.
  - Theodosius I banned the cult in decrees of 389–391 CE, and the Serapeum in Alexandria was destroyed by Christians in 391–392 CE.
- The speaker then reads a purported letter from Emperor Hadrian to his brother-in-law Servianus, dated 134 CE, citing *Historia Augusta* Vol. 3, pp. 399–406.
  - Key quoted sentence: "There those who worship Serapis are, in fact, Christians, and those who call themselves bishops of Christ are, in fact, devotees of Serapis."
- The speaker's argument: there were already people in Rome called "Christians" because they believed in Serapis as an "anointed one" (*christos*). When Jewish messianic faith arrived, the label stuck to everyone under one umbrella, swallowing both pagan and Jewish believers.
- He concludes: "Christian" is not a name or title the Father gave to his people.

## 4. Greek Philosophy as the Real Foundation (lines 150–258)

- The speaker asks whether Romans were waiting for a Messiah before Christianity arrived. Answer: not in the Hebrew sense.
- However, Roman culture was thoroughly saturated with *Greek philosophy*, which became the lens through which Gentiles understood the Hebrew Messiah.
- He makes the central thesis statement of the episode: "The foundation of Christianity is not built upon the foundation of the Hebrews, but the foundation of Greek philosophy."
- He contrasts Hebrew prophetic tradition (Jeremiah, Isaiah, Ezekiel) with Greek intellectual culture, arguing Romans did not adopt Jesus because they suddenly respected the Hebrew prophets.
- A modern analogy is offered: imagine Americans, believing they are the greatest nation, hearing about miracles in a small African nation like Namibia — they would not drop their culture and follow Namibia's. Instead they would appropriate the belief into their own framework. This, he argues, is what Rome did to the Hebrew faith.
- The speaker quotes Paul in Acts 24:14, noting Paul called the faith "the Way," a sect of the Yahudim, not "Christianity."
- When Gentile converts grew in number, the faith ceased to be a Jewish sect and became a Gentile religion reinterpreted through Greek philosophy.
- The speaker briefly addresses Pauline controversy: he does not reject Paul but says Paul was later misused by Gentile theologians who twisted his gospel into Greek philosophical categories.

## 5. Acts 17: Paul Preaches to Epicurean and Stoic Philosophers (lines 259–367)

- The speaker reads Acts 17:16–34 in full.
- Paul is provoked by Athens's idolatry. He reasons in the synagogue, marketplace, and finally at the Areopagus (Mars Hill).
- He cites the altar "To the Unknown God" as a bridge: "Him I proclaim to you."
- The audience includes Epicurean and Stoic philosophers (v. 18), some calling him a "babbler" (*spermologos*). Others want to hear more.
- When Paul mentions the resurrection, some mock, while others (Dionysius the Areopagite, Damaris, and others) believe.
- The speaker's exegetical point: these converts believed in Yahushua, but not necessarily according to Hebrew scripture truth. They believed through the filter of their existing Greek philosophical frameworks.

## 6. Clement of Alexandria: Greek Philosophy as "Pedagogue to Christ" (lines 368–499)

- Greek philosophy's influence on the U.S. is mentioned (democracy, architecture, founding fathers studying Plato, Aristotle, and Epicurus).
- The speaker quotes Clement of Alexandria extensively on philosophy as a preparatory teacher for the Greeks:
  - "In the advent of the Lord, philosophy was necessary to the Greeks for righteousness... Perhaps, too, philosophy was given to the Greeks directly and primarily until the Lord will call the Greeks. For this was a pedagogue to bring the Hellenic mind to Christ, as the law did the Hebrews."
  - "Hellenic philosophy does not comprehend the whole extent of the truth. Besides, it is destitute of strength to perform the commandments of the Lord. Yet, it prepares the way for the truly royal teaching."
  - "Philosophy is not, then, the product of vice, since it makes men virtuous. It follows, then, that it is the work of God."
- The speaker identifies Clement as a 2nd-century head of the Catechetical School of Alexandria, and emphasizes that Clement's most famous student was Origen.
- Origen is described as a major theologian who contributed to the development of the Trinity and ransom theory of atonement.
- The speaker reads Jeremiah 10:2 and Deuteronomy 12:29–32 as divine commands *against* learning the ways of the Gentiles.
- He argues Clement directly violates these commands by sanctifying Greek philosophy as a teacher toward Christ.

## 7. Plato and the Hellenization of the Messiah (lines 500–639)

- Plato is introduced as an ancient Greek philosopher (5th c. BCE), student of Socrates, teacher of Aristotle, founder of the Academy, and an aristocrat claiming descent from Poseidon.
- The speaker claims that when Christianity formed, Roman philosophers went back to Plato and tied his philosophy to their understanding of the Hebrew Messiah.
- He cites Plato's *Republic*, written c. 375 BCE, as describing "the murder of the just man" who was the embodiment of good, true, and beautiful — and that early Christians saw this as preparing Greeks for acceptance of their Messiah.
- Clement of Alexandria is identified as Plato's favorite among church fathers.
- The speaker asserts that because of philosophers like Plato, pagan Greeks believed the Hebrew Messiah was the same figure they had been philosophically prepared for.
- He concludes the Greeks seized the label "Christos" (anointed one) and made the Messiah a Greek figure first, before he was ever seen as Hebrew.
- The name became Hellenized ("Iesous" / Jesus), and the Hebrew name was removed. He claims they knew the name was Hebrew (Matthew was written in Hebrew, per church fathers) but deliberately rejected Hebrew culture.
- Another Clement quote: "He made a new covenant with us. For what belonged to the Greeks and Jews is old. But we, who worship him in a new way, in the third form, are Christians... the one and only God was known by the Greeks in a Gentile way, by the Jews in a Jewish way, and by us in a new and spiritual way."
- The speaker calls this "completely unbiblical" and cites Colossians 2:8: "Beware lest anyone cheat you through philosophy and empty deceit, according to the tradition of men, according to the basic principles of the world, and not according to Messiah."

## 8. Christianity as a "Religion of Tares": Rejection of Caesar and Persecution (lines 640–709)

- The speaker concedes that not *all* Gentile converts were false believers; he names Polycarp as a true believer.
- However, he argues that over time, it was *not* the influence of sincere Jewish believers or even sincere Gentile converts that defined Christianity.
- Christians clashed with Greco-Roman paganism, rejecting worship of Caesar and other gods, which led to Roman persecution.
- He argues persecution made Christianity look valid ("martyrs prove truth") but the faith that spread was not based on pure obedience to the Father.
- He reiterates the Parable of the Tares: Satan planted tares (false wheat) among the true seed at the very beginning of Gentile Christianity, mixing Greek philosophy into the Hebrew faith.
- He previews Part 8, which will cover the early church fathers (2nd–4th centuries) and their foundational doctrines.

## 9. Conclusion: Christianity is a Greek Religion (lines 710–769)

- The speaker restates his thesis: "Christianity is a faith of the Greeks. Not the Hebrews."
- He says everything in Christianity references Greek culture first, Hebrew culture not at all.
- He warns the audience that on the surface Christianity seems biblical, but its doctrines abuse grace, edit covenants, and reject the way the Father chose to reach humanity.
- Call to action: come out of the "religion of tares" and come to the Father in truth.
- Urges viewers to proceed to Part 8.
