# Episode 14 — The Maccabean Revolt: The War Against Hellenization

> **Source**: `14 - TESTING_CHRISTIANITY_PT._14_THE_MACCABEAN_REVOLT_-_THE_WAR_AGAINST_HELLENIZATION.txt` (~36 min)

## Overview
This episode argues that the Hellenization begun under Alexander the Great was not merely an ancient cultural shift but an ongoing spiritual threat—one that the Jews of Judea actively resisted through armed revolt. The speaker uses the history of the Maccabean Revolt (c. 167–164 BCE) to frame modern Christianity as a continuation of Greek cultural imperialism, asserting that the Jewish people’s violent rejection of Hellenization in the 2nd century BCE provides the essential context for understanding Jesus’ ministry and why, in the speaker’s view, mainstream Christianity has “hijacked” a Hebrew faith.

---

## Detailed Summary

### 1. The continuity of Hellenization (0:00–0:44)
- The speaker opens by stating that the period of Hellenization did not end with Alexander the Great. Greek culture, he argues, continues to evolve through modern Western civilization—from the Romans to “Masonic England”—and remains the foundational framework of the contemporary world.
- He contrasts this with the unchanging Law of Yahuwah, which Satan wants humanity to reject.

### 2. Sources cited (0:44–1:24)
- The speaker explicitly names his historical sources: the Books of the Maccabees, Solomon Grazel’s *The History of the Jews*, and Flavius Josephus’ *Antiquities of the Jews*.
- He frames the coming history as vital for understanding the people Jesus came to and the origins of Christianity.

### 3. Jewish resistance to Hellenization (1:24–3:21)
- **Economic context**: The 3rd century BCE saw Greek culture dominate trade and prosperity. Jews in Judea, the speaker says, were considered peasants—mostly farmers, small merchants, and artisans—content with their heritage and uninterested in blending in.
- **Cultural contrast**: The Jews of Judea are described as never having contact with the highest Greek civilizations (Athens, Alexandria) and, even if they had, would have rejected Greek philosophy and poetry as inferior because they alone were in covenant with Yahuwah.
- **Upper-class pressure**: Upper-class priestly families, exporters, and importers in Jerusalem wanted to make the city a trading center. To do so, they argued for superficial Hellenizing reforms—Greek dress, Greek games, a Greek constitution—without abandoning their faith entirely. The speaker compares this to Israel’s demand for a king in 1 Samuel.

### 4. The Tobiads and early Hellenizing influence (3:21–3:57)
- The main advocates of Hellenization in Judea are identified as the **Tobiads**, a family possibly descended from **Tobias the Ammonite** (Nehemiah 13). Though not originally Jewish, they intermingled with priestly families and became politically influential.
- Around **230 BCE**, **Joseph ben Tobias** secured from the Ptolemaic king of Egypt the right to collect taxes across Syria (including Judea), using this to centralize trade in Jerusalem.

### 5. Syrian takeover and internal Jewish division (3:57–5:44)
- The episode distinguishes this period from both Old and New Testament history, calling it “wildly important” for context.
- After Alexander’s empire split between the **Ptolemies** (Egypt) and **Seleucids** (Syria), **Antiochus III (“the Great”)** forced Egypt to relinquish Palestine by **198 BCE**.
- Jews divided: some favored Seleucid annexation; others (including **Hyrcanus**, son of Joseph ben Tobias) favored Egypt because of tax revenue.
- The common people, the speaker says, didn’t care about the political shift until it threatened their worship.

### 6. Heliodorus and the Temple treasury (5:44–6:32)
- Pro-Seleucid Jews revealed to **Seleucus IV** that money for their Egyptian opponents was stored in the Temple.
- Seleucus sent his official **Heliodorus** to confiscate it.
- According to tradition (2 Maccabees 3), Heliodorus was confronted in the Temple by a horseman and beaten nearly to death, leaving empty-handed.
- This event deepened the rift: Jews saw that Hellenized Jews would stop at nothing, even sending Gentiles to rob the Temple.

### 7. Antiochus IV Epiphanes and the crackdown (6:32–8:19)
- **Seleucus IV** died and was succeeded by his brother **Antiochus IV Epiphanes**.
- Antiochus sought to reunite Alexander’s empire and attached himself to Greek culture, taking the surname **Epiphanes** (“manifest/visible god”) and identifying himself with **Jupiter/Zeus**.
- Since Judea resisted Hellenization most strongly, a group of Hellenized Jews proposed removing the sitting high priest and inserting their own candidate, **Jason**, in exchange for money. Antiochus agreed and promised Jerusalem a Greek constitution and the right to coin money.
- The common people were outraged: this was the first time a foreign government had interfered in high-priestly succession.

### 8. Jason, Menelaus, and the spread of Greek culture (8:19–9:18)
- Under Jason, gymnasiums were built in Jerusalem; young priests neglected Temple duties to play sports. Greek dress, names, and language became fashionable.
- The speaker emphasizes this new culture was distracting and led to moral looseness.
- The Hellenizers were not satisfied with the pace. **Jason** was forced to flee, and **Menelaus**—not even from a priestly family—was installed as high priest.

### 9. Antiochus’s decrees and martyrdom (9:18–11:31)
- Antiochus, enraged by Jewish resistance, resolved to destroy their culture and force them to become Greek. He outlawed the Torah.
- A Syrian army marched into Jerusalem (on the **25th of Kislev**), killed many, and enforced edicts: no Sabbath, no feast days, no circumcision. Jews were forced to eat pork in the Temple.
- A statue of **Jupiter** (bearing resemblance to Antiochus) was placed on the altar; pigs were sacrificed before it.
- A group called the **Hasidim** (“pious ones”) emerged, choosing martyrdom over violation of Torah. The speaker then reads extensively from **2 and 4 Maccabees**, describing the torture and death of the elderly priest **Eleazar** and the **seven brothers and their mother** who refused to eat unclean meat.

### 10. The Hasmonean revolt begins (11:31–14:38)
- Thousands fled to the hills. Hellenized Jews allegedly informed on the Hasidim to the Greeks.
- Hasidim accepted martyrdom without fighting. Other Jews did resist, but under Hasidic influence some would not defend themselves on the Sabbath—allowing Greeks to attack them on that day.
- In **Modin**, northwest of Jerusalem, the priestly **Hasmonean** family was led by the aged **Mattathias**, father of five sons: Simon, Eliezer, **Yehudah** (Judah), Yohanan, and Yohanathan.
- Syrian soldiers set up an altar in Modin’s marketplace, produced a pig, and ordered Mattathias to sacrifice it to Jupiter. A Hellenized Jew stepped forward to do it.
- Mattathias snatched the captain’s sword, killed the apostate Jew, then the captain. His sons and other Jews rushed the soldiers, killed them, and destroyed the altar.
- Mattathias cried, **“Whoever is for Yahuwah, let him come unto me”**—a call that echoed across Judea.

### 11. Judah Maccabee and the guerrilla war (14:38–17:54)
- Mattathias and his followers abandoned their homes and began attacking small Syrian troops and Hellenized Jewish communities.
- Mattathias died around **167 BCE** (within a year of the revolt). Leadership passed to **Yehudah**, who acquired the surname **Maccabee** (possibly from Hebrew/Aramaic for “hammer”).
- Under Judah’s leadership, the outnumbered Maccabees began winning. Local Syrian commanders, including Hellenized Jews, repeatedly failed to corner them.

### 12. Syrian reaction and the road to Jerusalem (17:54–19:33)
- Antiochus realized he had a full rebellion. He appointed **Lysias** to crush it while he himself went east to fight a Parthian revolt.
- Lysias underestimated the Maccabees and sent smaller forces rather than a full army. Judah, knowing the terrain, ambushed and annihilated them at night.
- The speaker calls this the **“Battle of Amos”** (not a standard name in major sources).
- With the victory, the road to Jerusalem opened. The Maccabees approached the city—held by the enemy for nearly three years—with songs of praise.

### 13. Cleansing and rededication of the Temple (19:33–21:31)
- **Menelaus**, the Hellenizers, and pagan residents fled Jerusalem.
- The peasants-turned-soldiers cleansed the Temple, removed every sign of paganism, set aside the stones of the defiled altar, and erected a new one.
- Exactly three years after the abomination was introduced, on **25 Kislev 165 BCE**, the Temple was rededicated.
- The speaker says this is the origin of **Hanukkah** (“Feast of Dedication”), an eight-day celebration. He notes that **John 10:22–23** records Jesus at the Feast of Dedication.
- He emphasizes: without this victory, the Jewish people would have been assimilated into Greek culture, and “we would not have been able to have our Messiah.”

### 14. Interlude: Modern Hasidim vs. ancient Hasidim (21:31–22:15)
- The speaker briefly discusses modern **Hasidic Judaism**, noting they wear 18th-century Eastern European clothing (black coats, fur hats).
- He claims they appropriated the name “Hasidim” from the Maccabean-era group but are not the same. He identifies their founder as **Rabbi Israel Ben Eliezer (Baal Shem Tov)**, an 18th-century mystic and practitioner of Kabbalah, and says this exposes a hijacking of the original name.

### 15. Theological polemic: Hellenization then and now (22:15–25:26)
- Returning to his main thesis, the speaker argues that the history of the Maccabean revolt demonstrates a total Jewish rejection of Greek culture.
- He quotes **John 7:33–36**, where Jesus’ listeners ask if he intends to go to “the dispersion among the Greeks.” The speaker argues this shows the Judean Jews’ disdain for the Hellenized diaspora—they assumed Jesus was going somewhere they would never follow.
- He claims **Paul’s** statement “there is no difference between Jew and Greek” has been misread by modern Christians who think Paul was speaking about pagan Greeks, when he was actually addressing Hellenized Jews.
- He compares the Hellenization of the Messiah (changing his name to Greek, adapting his culture) to a hypothetical Chinese takeover of America rewriting Donald Trump as Chinese.

### 16. Christianity as modern Hellenism (25:26–29:32)
- The speaker concludes that mainstream Christianity has continued the Hellenizing project Antiochus began.
- He argues that Christians identify with the Greek “Christos” rather than the Hebrew “Messiah,” and that this is why the church rejected Hebrew roots.
- He frames Christianity as a Greek-influenced movement that removed the Hebrew context necessary to understand Jesus’ ministry.

### 17. Transition to Part 15 (29:32–30:01)
- The speaker announces that the next episode will cover the Church Fathers and how they blended Hebrew scripture with Greek philosophy, leading to the Trinity.
- He ends with his recurring thesis: Christianity is a “religion of tares” that lacks obedience, reverence, and truth, and urges viewers to “come out of this religion” and “come to our Father in truth.”
