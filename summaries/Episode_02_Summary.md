# Episode 2 — Summary: "But the Word Christian Is in My Bible"

Source: `transcripts/02 - TESTING_CHRISTIANITY_PT._2_BUT_THE_WORD_CHRISTIAN_IS_IN_MY_BIBLE.txt`

## Frame & Opening
- The speaker recaps Part 1 and reiterates that the video is *not* an attack on biblical faith but a distinction between what is biblical and what is religious.
- Core thesis restated: **Christianity is not synonymous with the Bible.** Speaking against Christianity is not the same as speaking against the Bible.
  - "If you feel that way, you are wrong."
  - "The actual truth is that Christianity is against the Bible."
  - "I believe in the Bible fully, all of the Bible, no exclusions."
- Christianity is a religion claiming to be centered on the Bible; if that correlation were direct, the series would not exist.

## The "Majority" Definition of Christianity
- The speaker relays a conversation with a brother whose definition of "Christian" is representative of mainstream belief:
  - A Christian is someone who has repented of sin, put faith in Jesus Christ (Yahusha), trusts in his atonement and resurrection, is filled with the Holy Spirit, and is sealed until the day of redemption.
  - This brother also defines a Christian as "a Gentile who got to get in because Israel has been blinded for a season."
- The speaker says he has "no problem with this definition for the most part" and it is not what makes him oppose Christianity.
- **The deeper problem**: that definition represents what the apostles actually practiced, which is better called **"the Way"** — not Christianity.

## Historical Gap: From "The Way" to Christianity
- After the apostles were killed, there is "about a 300 year gap of history that is not widely known" in regard to "the Way."
- At some point, the main spreading of Christianity was no longer done by those following "the Way."
- The Hebrews (Yahudim / Jews) were "removed out of the picture of spreading the Way."
- Later, **Rome made Christianity their main religion** — the speaker states that without this historical move, 2.5 billion people today would not be Christians.
- Christianity is the biggest religion in the world; the audience must understand how it became this.

## Stumbling Block: The Word "Christian" in Scripture
- The speaker addresses the objection that the word "Christian" appears in the King James Bible.
- Two key passages cited:
  - **1 Peter 4:16** — "Yet if anyone suffers as a Christian, let him not be ashamed, but let him glorify God in this manner."
  - **Acts 11:26** — "The disciples were first called Christians in Antioch."
- The speaker argues these are translations using Greek influence, not Hebrew influence.

### Peter Did Not Write "Christian"
- Peter's actual name was **Kepha**; he was from the tribe of Judah, a Hebrew who spoke Hebrew, not Greek.
- **Evidence**: Mark was with Peter as his interpreter.
  - Cites **Irenaeus**: "Mark, the interpreter and follower of Peter, begins his gospel narrative..."
  - Cites **Clement of Alexandria**: Mark wrote entirely what is called the Gospel according to Mark after following Peter.
  - If Peter spoke Greek or Latin, he would not have needed an interpreter.
  - Therefore Peter "definitely did not write his epistle in Greek."
- The speaker frames this as part of the larger problem of Christianity wanting to be part of the blessing while misappropriating Hebrew culture and applying **Hellenization** to the faith.
  - "The Bible is a book about one culture and one people, Yasharel, the sons of Jacob."
  - Christians want to take the covenant but "ignore that culture and rebrand it for themselves."

### The Maccabean Precedent
- The speaker references the **Book of Maccabees** (not included in Protestant Bibles).
  - Before the time of Messiah, there was a war between the Yahudim and the Greeks.
  - Some Yahudim bowed to Greece, assimilated, adapted to **Hellenism** (spreading of Greek culture), and rejected Yahudim culture.
  - The Yahudim who fought and won rejected those assimilated Jews and called them "Greeks or Gentiles."
  - These assimilated Jews were in synagogues in Asia Minor, Greece, and Spain that Paul visited.
  - The Yahudim in Rome that Peter went to were slaves taken by **Titus** when Rome first conquered Judea, not the assimilated ones.

### Josephus on Jewish Language Attitudes
- The speaker quotes **Josephus**, *Antiquities of the Jews* (Book 20, last chapter), written in Greek around 93–94 CE after the Temple was destroyed.
  - Josephus says he "took a great deal of pains to obtain the learning of the Greeks" but "cannot pronounce Greek with sufficient exactness."
  - Crucial quote: "Our nation does not encourage those that learn the languages of many nations."
  - This was the mindset of a Jew 20 years after the Temple was destroyed.
  - The culture of the Jews did not encourage learning foreign languages.
- The speaker argues: "You cannot understand this people while ignoring their culture. But this is exactly what Christianity does."

## Etymology: Christos vs. Mashiach
- When dealing with 1 Peter 4:16, the speaker argues Peter did not write "Christian" because:
  - "Christian" comes from **Christos** in Greek.
  - Peter would have said **Mashiach** (Hebrew for Messiah / anointed one).
  - It would be better translated **Messianus** rather than Christian.
- The reason Rome uses Christos instead of Mashiach is **syncretism** — attaching to Greek pagan culture.
  - The Greek word **Christos** means "anointed one" or "chosen one" — this is the Greek pagan cultural concept of an anointed one.
  - By using Christos, "you are attaching with their pagan culture."
- Similarly, the name **Jesus** is a Greek adaptation that does not match **Yahushua** in transliteration from Hebrew.
  - "They adapted Greek culture to the faith very subtly and made the faith adapt to the Greeks instead of the Hebrew influence that it actually came from."

## Acts 11:26 — What Others Called Them
- Even setting aside the Hebrew-vs.-Greek linguistic argument, the speaker argues Acts 11:26 says the disciples "were called" Christians in Antioch.
  - This is what **outsiders** called them, not what they called themselves.
  - "Just because they were called this does not supersede the rest of the Bible and how our Father made the new covenant."
- What the apostles **actually** called themselves:
  - **Acts 9:1–2**: Paul asked for letters to Damascus so he could arrest "any who were of **the way**."
  - **Acts 24:14**: Paul says, "According to **the way**, which they call a sect, so I worship the Elohim of my fathers."
  - Paul did not say "I persecuted Christians"; he said "I persecuted **this way**."
- The speaker argues there was a "direct effort to remove the influence that Yahweh had on his people, and they merged in the already pagan culture in with the culture of the Hebrews."
  - This was done through doctrine and through language.

## Peter's Allegiance to Hebrew Culture
- The speaker cites **Galatians 2**, where Paul confronted Peter for withdrawing and separating from the Gentiles.
  - "To think that this man, who walked with Yahushua and denied him three times, who cut off the ear of a Roman soldier, to think that he later rejected the culture of his people to attach to the culture of those he deemed as sellouts is ridiculous."
- Because Christian teachers "do not stand for the word in this way," this argument has never been presented to the audience before.
- **Conclusion on the label**: Just because outsiders called them Christians, "that's not what the apostles called themselves. They said they were of **the Way**."
  - "How come you're not a follower of the Way?"
  - "This is the actual faith that I'm protecting. Christianity is not the Way. It's just masquerading as so."

## Preview of Part 3

  - "Did he prophesy that there would be a version of his truth that would be planted in this world that would look like it was his until it grew in fullness? Yeah, he absolutely did."
  - This refers to the **Parable of the Wheat and Tares** (Matthew 13:24–43), which becomes the central thesis of Part 3.
- Final summary of Christianity's character:
  - On the surface it seems biblical, but its doctrines are not.
  - It is a doctrine of "abusing grace, editing covenants, rejecting the way our Father actually chose to reach humanity, and making it only be about the way Christians want to receive him."
  - "It lacks the love, obedience, and reverence for our Father that he deserves."
  - "You must come out of this religion of tares and come to our Father in truth."
