- The speaker describes Pythagoras's travels: born on Samos, traveled with his merchant father, lived in Egypt where he studied religion with Egyptian priests.

## The Peloponnesian War and the Rise of the Sophists

- Nearly a century after Pythagoras, Athens and Sparta fought the Peloponnesian War.
- The war weakened Athens significantly, shifted power to Sparta, and caused economic hardship and social disruption.
- A new philosophy emerged among a generation that had become "soft and spoiled" by Athens's prior success and then suffered defeat.
- People began questioning "absolute good." A new movement taught that virtue was defined by personal desire and feeling, leading to materialism.
- These philosophers were called **sophists** — traveling teachers of rhetoric, philosophy, and politics to wealthy young men, focusing on persuasive argumentation for a fee.
- Sophism gained a negative connotation: intellectually dishonest arguments that prioritize winning over truth.
- The speaker connects modern Christian debate culture to the sophists, arguing debate is not a Hebrew mindset but a Greek one.
- He claims the Pharisees and Sadducees were influenced by Greek thought to debate about Yahushua, which led to the rebellion that crucified him.
- The sophists allegedly believed everything was atoms, the universe resulted from blind chance, all religions were human inventions, and some promoted atheism.

## Socrates (c. 470–399 BC)

- Socrates is presented as a philosopher who stood against the sophists by arguing for objective standards of good and evil.
- He is called the "founder of Western philosophy" and one of the first moral philosophers.
- Socrates allegedly left no original writings; his legacy is known through his students, especially Plato.
- The speaker claims Socrates was influenced by Pythagoras and believed in reincarnation (transmigration of souls).
- Socrates taught that the virtuous person controls thoughts and emotions through philosophy and that doing good leads to true happiness.
- He was unpopular in Athens and eventually accused, tried, and forced to commit suicide by drinking hemlock when he refused to denounce his philosophy.
- Socrates said he did not fear death because philosophy rejects material things to prepare the soul for the afterlife.
- The speaker notes that philosophy before Socrates is called "pre-Socratic" and that after Socrates, philosophy shifted toward individual ethics and virtue.
- Socrates' contribution: moving intellectual pursuits from physical science to abstract ethics and morality.
- Socrates is credited with bringing the concept of "good and evil" into Greek thought where it had not existed before.

Socrates asks whether morality is good because the gods love it, or whether the gods love it because it is independently good. The apologist presents the Christian solution: God is intrinsically good and morality participates in his eternal nature.
- The speaker rejects this, arguing that "morals" are Greek and mutable, while Hebrews followed Torah (divine law), which does not change.
- Socrates' *daimonion*: In Plato's *Apology*, Socrates claimed to have a "daimonian" — an inner divine voice warning him against mistakes but never telling him what to do. The speaker translates this as "a demon" and says Socrates was guided by a demon, and therefore Christianity is influenced by a demon-guided man.

## Plato (427–347 BC)

- Plato was Socrates' most famous student. He recorded Socrates' ideas in dialogues using the Socratic method (questioning to lead others to conclusions).
- The lineage is traced: Thales → Anaximander → Pythagoras → Socrates → Plato.
- The speaker claims each philosopher passed down mystery-religion ideas.
- Plato's philosophy:
  - Two realms of existence: mind/spirit and matter.
  - The realm of mind contains ideals or archetypes (Forms).
  - The **Demiurge**: Plato's creator god, the "craftsman," identified with the Form of the Good.
  - Theory of Forms: perfect, eternal, changeless concepts existing outside space and time. The Form of the Good is the highest form and source of all others.
- Plato's cosmology (from *Timaeus* and Republic): After the Demiurge made the universe, he created the Olympian deities; human souls first inhabited heaven but desired the material world, so lesser gods prepared bodies for them. The earth itself has a soul.
- Goal of human life: become virtuous through philosophy and reincarnation, returning to a divine state.
- **The "Just Man" and crucifixion**: The speaker quotes Republic Book 2, 361E–362A, where the perfectly just man will endure lashes, the rack, chains, branding, and "finally... he will be crucified." The speaker argues the "Jesus of the Greeks is just Plato's just man" and that early Christians accepted the Messiah through Greek philosophical categories rather than Hebrew prophecy.
- He calls Plato "like their Isaiah or Jeremiah" — the Greeks' scriptures and prophets.
- **The Allegory of the Cave**: From Republic. Prisoners in a cave see only shadows. The allegory illustrates idealism and how people understand only a small part of reality through senses. The speaker claims modern movies like *The Truman Show*, *The Matrix*, and *Interstellar* are retellings of Plato's cave, proving Greek philosophy pervades modern entertainment.
- **"One and the many"**: The Theory of Forms implies that a single perfect universal essence (the One) underlies all diverse manifestations (the Many). The speaker claims this is the primary philosophy Origen used to create the doctrine of the Trinity.
- Plato founded **the Academy** (c. 387 BC), described as the first university in the Western world.
- **Cosimo de' Medici**: In the Renaissance, Cosimo de' Medici was so inspired by Plato that he founded a Platonic Academy in Florence (with Marsilio Ficino).

## Aristotle (384–322 BC)

- Aristotle was Plato's student.
- He developed a system of logic and scientific exploration.
- He divided philosophy into three categories: mathematics, physics, and metaphysics.
- He taught that practical philosophy concerns ethics and politics, while poetic philosophy dealt with metaphysics.
- He developed virtue ethics, emphasizing cultivation of good character traits to achieve happiness.
- He began the Aristotelian tradition that set groundwork for modern science.
- Until the Renaissance, the modern world was most influenced by Aristotle (as stated at the opening).
- Western philosophy, including occult philosophy, has roots in Aristotle and Plato.
