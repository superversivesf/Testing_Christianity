# Episode 15 — The 8th Day: The Reason Why People Go to Church on Sunday

> **Source**: `15 - TESTING_CHRISTIANITY_PT._15_THE_8TH_DAY_-_THE_REASON_WHY_PEOPLE_GO_TO_CHURCH_ON_SUNDAY.txt` (~39 min)

## Overview

This episode is the most evidence-dense installment of the series. The speaker argues that Sunday worship in Christianity is not rooted in apostolic teaching but in the doctrines of 2nd-century church fathers—above all Justin Martyr and Clement of Alexandria—who rejected Sabbath observance and replaced it with an "eighth day" (Sunday) theology. Using direct quotations from patristic writings and the Roman Catholic Catechism, he traces what he calls a break from Hebrew scripture through Greek philosophical reinterpretation, concluding that modern Christians follow a man-made tradition whose origins most believers do not recognize.

## Detailed Summary

### 1. Framing: history as the test of influence (0:00–1:44)
- The speaker opens by recapping Part 14 (Maccabean Revolt) and reiterates his core thesis: mainstream Christianity is the product of a historical break between the Hebrew apostles and the later Gentile church fathers.
- He argues that the exclusion of the Books of Maccabees from Protestant Bibles contributes to ignorance of Jewish resistance to Hellenization and leads to misreadings of Paul (e.g., "there is no difference between Jew and Greek").
- He defends the use of historical sources alongside scripture, quoting Winston Churchill: "History is written by the victors."

### 2. The "tree" of Christian influence (1:44–6:30)
- Using a tree metaphor, the speaker traces the lineage of American Christianity backward:
  - **Leaf/branch**: The individual believer, influenced by parents and local church.
  - **Denominational branches**: Most U.S. pastors trained in seminaries; denominations (Puritan, Presbyterian, Baptist, Methodist, Pentecostal, Adventist, Jehovah's Witnesses) all trace back to Protestant roots.
  - **English branch**: King James Bible culture, English Reformation, Methodist and Presbyterian origins.
  - **German branch**: Protestant Reformation under Martin Luther (16th century), with offshoots like Calvinism (Switzerland/Netherlands) and Baptist tradition.
  - **Roman Catholic trunk**: The speaker argues Catholicism is the foundation of Protestantism; without Rome, there would be no Protestant Church.
  - **Roots**: He claims the roots are not the Jewish apostles but the early church fathers, whom he calls "tares" planted by Satan through Greek philosophy.
- The speaker insists that every modern Christian, whether Catholic or Protestant, is historically tethered to this tree and its Greco-Roman roots.

### 3. Justin Martyr's conversion and philosophy (6:30–13:05)
- The speaker profiles **Justin Martyr** (c. 100–165 CE), citing his *Dialogue with Trypho*.
- Justin's pre-Christian education in Stoic, Peripatetic (Aristotelian), Pythagorean, and finally Platonic philosophy is described in detail.
- Justin's conversion story: while meditating by the sea, an old man convinced him to study the Hebrew prophets, after which Justin concluded that "true philosophy" was found in Christ and the prophets.
- Despite conversion, Justin continued to wear his philosopher's cloak and traveled widely as a teacher.
- The speaker emphasizes Justin's continued reverence for philosophy: "Without philosophy and right reason, none can possibly have practical wisdom" (*Dialogue*, ch. 3, p. 7 in the PDF referenced).
- The key point: Justin Martyr, a former pagan philosopher, became an influential early Christian voice who shaped doctrine from a Greek philosophical framework.

### 4. Justin Martyr on Sunday worship and rejection of the Sabbath (13:05–16:35)
- The speaker quotes **Justin Martyr, *First Apology*, chapter 67** ("Weekly Worship of the Christians"):
  - "But Sunday is the day on which we all hold our common assembly, because it is the first day on which God, having wrought a change in the darkness and matter, made the world. And Jesus Christ, our Savior, on the same day rose from the dead. For he was crucified on the day before that of Saturn, Saturday, and on that day after that of Saturn, which is the day of the sun, having appeared to his apostles and disciples."
- The speaker argues this is historical evidence that 2nd-century Christians were already observing Sunday, not the Sabbath, and doing so explicitly because of creation and resurrection symbolism.
- He then quotes **Justin Martyr, *Dialogue with Trypho*, chapter 10** (p. 20):
  - Justin lists what Trypho (a Jew) finds objectionable about Christians: "that we do not live in accordance with the law, and do not circumcise the flesh as did your forefathers, and do not keep the Sabbath as you do."
- Trypho's response (ch. 10, p. 21):
  - "You, saying you worship God, and thinking yourself superior to other people, separate from them in no respect, and do not make your life different from the heathen, in that you keep neither the feasts, nor the Sabbaths, nor have circumcision... If therefore you have any defense to make with reference to these points... we would very gladly hear from you."
- The speaker stresses that the debates Christians have today about Sabbath and law are the same debates from the 2nd century.

### 5. Justin Martyr's "replacement theology" and the "new law" (16:35–20:30)
- The speaker quotes **Justin Martyr, *Dialogue with Trypho*, chapter 11** (p. 22):
  - "For the law given that orb is already antiquated and belongs to you alone, but that other belongs to all men absolutely. And a law set over against a law has made the one before it to cease... And as eternal and final law was Christ given to us, and this disposition is sure, after which there is no law or ordinance or command."
  - "For we are the true and spiritual Israelitish nation, and the race of Judah, and of Jacob, and Isaac, and Abraham... We, I say, are all this, who were brought nigh to God by him who was crucified, even Christ."
- The speaker identifies this as the origin of **replacement theology**: the church fathers taught that Christians, not ethnic Jews, had inherited the covenant.
- He also quotes **Justin Martyr, *Dialogue with Trypho*, chapter 12** (p. 25):
  - "The new law wishes you to keep Sabbath all the time, and you think you are acting piously by being lazy for one day... If any among you is a false swearer or thief, let him cease... Then he has kept the delightsome and true Sabbaths of God."
- Justin taught that Sabbath observance was a moral matter (ceasing from sin) rather than literal rest on the seventh day.

### 6. The "eighth day" theology (20:30–22:15)
- The speaker turns to Justin Martyr's numerology. In **Dialogue with Trypho, chapter 24** (p. 29):
  - "The eighth day had a certain mystical meaning which was proclaimed by God by these means, rather than the seventh."
- Justin connected the number eight to salvation: eight people saved on Noah's Ark, circumcision on the eighth day, and now Sunday as the "new eighth day."
- The speaker identifies this as philosophical/Pythagorean thinking.
- In **chapter 41** (p. 82), Justin writes:
  - "For the first day of the week, the first indeed of all the days there ever were, is further called the eighth, according to the number of all the days of their cycle, and thus it ever remains the first."
- The speaker argues this is the theological origin of calling Sunday the "Lord's day" and the "eighth day."

### 7. Clement of Alexandria on Plato and the eighth day (22:15–23:35)
- The speaker introduces **Clement of Alexandria** (late 2nd century) and notes that he, too, blended Greek philosophy with Christian faith.
- In **Clement's *Stromata* (Miscellanies), Book 5, page 777**, the speaker quotes:
  - "And the Lord's day, Plato prophetically speaks of in the tenth book of the Republic, in these words: 'And when seven days have passed to each of them in the meadow, on the eighth, they are to set out and arrive in four days.'"
- The speaker argues that Clement was so committed to the "eighth day" idea that he claimed **Plato himself had prophesied Sunday** in the *Republic*.
- The speaker notes that Christians today call it the "Lord's day" rather than the "eighth day," but the underlying doctrine is the same: Sunday has replaced the Sabbath.

### 8. Roman Catholic Catechism on Sunday (23:35–25:20)
- The speaker turns to the **Catechism of the Catholic Church**, calling it the place where "the roots formed into the trunk of the tree."
- He quotes **CCC §2174**:
  - "Jesus rose from the dead on the first day of the week. Because it is the first day, the day of Christ's resurrection recalls the first creation. Because it is the eighth day, following the Sabbath, it symbolizes the new creation ushered in by Christ's resurrection. For Christians it has become the first of all days, the first of all feasts, the Lord's day, Sunday. We all gather on the day of the sun, for it is the first day, after the Jewish Sabbath, but also the first day, when God, separating matter from darkness, made the world. And on this same day, Jesus Christ, our Savior, rose from the dead. Sunday, fulfillment of the Sabbath."
- He then quotes **CCC §2175**:
  - "Sunday is expressly distinguished from the Sabbath, which it follows chronologically every week. For Christians, its ceremonial observance replaces that of the Sabbath... Those who lived according to the old order of things have come to a new hope, no longer keeping the Sabbath, but the Lord's day."
- The speaker concludes that the Catholic Church formally teaches Sunday observance as a replacement for Sabbath, rooted in patristic doctrine rather than in any explicit command from the apostles.

### 9. Rebuttal of Acts 20:7 as a Sunday proof-text (25:20–26:55)
- The speaker anticipates the common Christian counter-argument: **Acts 20:7** ("On the first day of the week, when the disciples came together to break bread...").
- He argues this is not a doctrinal statement replacing the Sabbath; it is a narrative detail about a meeting before Paul's departure.
- He insists that no scripture explicitly commands abandoning the Sabbath and keeping Sunday instead.

### 10. Biblical commands for Sabbath observance (26:55–29:15)
- The speaker cites the **Sabbath commandment** from **Exodus 20:8–11** and **Genesis 2:3**.
- He then quotes **Isaiah 56:6–8** to show that even foreigners/Gentiles who join themselves to Yahuwah are expected to keep the Sabbath:
  - "Also the sons of the stranger who joined themselves to Yahuwah to serve him and to love the name of Yahuwah... everyone who keeps from defiling the Sabbath holds fast my covenant. Even them I will bring to my holy mountain... for my house shall be called a house of prayer for all nations."
- His conclusion: rejecting the Sabbath is not biblical; it is a doctrine of men passed down from the church fathers.

### 11. Conclusion and challenge to viewers (29:15–31:00)
- The speaker reiterates that Christians go to church on Sunday because of the early church fathers' doctrines, not because of scripture.
- He challenges viewers to ask their Christian friends and family:
  - Why do you go to church on Sunday?
  - What verse tells you not to keep the Sabbath anymore?
  - Do you know what the "eighth day" is?
  - Where does "the Lord's day" come from in scripture?
- He says if he can prove Sunday observance is not found in the scriptures but comes from doctrines of men, will they be willing to listen?
- He announces that **Part 16 will cover the Trinity**, another doctrine he attributes to the church fathers.

### 12. Outro (31:00–end)
- The speaker repeats his thesis: Christianity on the surface seems biblical, but its doctrines (Sunday worship, Trinity, etc.) are man-made traditions rooted in Greek philosophy.
- He urges viewers to "come out of this religion of tares and come to our Father in truth."
- He thanks donors and directs viewers to the next video in the playlist.
