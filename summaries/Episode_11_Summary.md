# Episode 11 — The Road to the Trinity: You Must Understand Greek Philosophy

**Source:** `transcripts/11 - TESTING_CHRISTIANITY_PT._11_THE_ROAD_TO_THE_TRINITY_-_YOU_MUST_UNDERSTAND_GREEK_PHILOSOPHY.txt`  
**Runtime:** ~25 min  
**Series:** Testing Christianity (Part 11 of 15)

## Purpose of the Episode
This episode sets up the later discussion of the Trinity by introducing Greek philosophy as the dominant conceptual framework the speaker believes Christianity was built upon, rather than Hebrew biblical thought. It provides a historical overview of ancient Greece, its religious beliefs, and the development of philosophy, arguing that without understanding this Greek influence, one cannot understand mainstream Christian doctrine.

## Detailed Bullet-Point Summary (in order of appearance)

### Introduction: Why Greek Philosophy Matters (0:00–1:20)
- The episode begins by stating that in order to understand Christianity (especially the Trinity), one must first understand Greek philosophy.
- Christianity is claimed to be a religion that *appears* biblical on the surface but is actually grounded in Greek philosophy.
- The speaker argues that people in Western nations (especially the United States) have been indoctrinated with a Greek philosophical mindset without realizing it.
- The goal is to identify and discern "the wheat and the tares" — the difference between the original Hebrew faith and the later Greek-influenced Christianity.

### Christianity vs. Hebrew Roots (1:20–3:00)
- The speaker recounts being labeled a member of the "Hebrew Roots cult."
- He argues this label reveals that Christianity is *not* rooted in Hebrews but in Greeks.
- The biblical covenant was made with Abraham, Isaac, and Jacob — all Hebrews. God did not make covenants with Greeks, Romans, or Anglo-Saxons.
- Therefore, rejecting "Hebrew Roots" means accepting that Christianity is built on Greek philosophy rather than Hebrew scripture.
- Christianity is described as "a religion of the Romans that is inspired by the Hebrews, but built around the philosophy of the Greeks."

### Modern Indoctrination and the Golden Age of Greece (3:00–5:45)
- The speaker claims that modern formal education does not truly educate but indoctrinates students into a Greek-influenced worldview.
- The number of college graduates is higher than ever, yet knowledge of Greek philosophy is surprisingly low despite Western civilization's deep Hellenistic roots.
- The speaker goes back in time to the 5th century BC, the period after the Greek victory in the Persian Wars.
- On a peninsula in the northern Mediterranean, the "children of Yavan" (Greeks/Hellenes), descended from Japheth, were growing in strength.
- Under Athens and Sparta, the Greeks defeated the Persian Empire (referencing the movie *300* as the time period).
- **Athenian Golden Age innovations:** architecture, medicine, writing, poetry, sculpture, dramas, comedic plays, astronomy, and government.
- Democracy's foundations are traced to this era: every male citizen was part of the **Ekklesia** (assembly), meeting ~40 times a year; the **Boule** (500 men) handled day-to-day government business.
- **Sigma Pi Phi** (also known as "the Boulé"), the first black Greek fraternity, is identified as being named after the Athenian Boule. The speaker calls its founders "sellout Negroes inspired by cultures who came to oppress and hijack their own people."
- Sparta developed a military culture; Athens became a trading and seafaring power with a strong navy.
- Greek city-states spread Hellenistic culture by colonizing towns on Aegean and Mediterranean shores.

### Greek Religion and Mythology (5:45–7:30)
- The Greeks believed in developing both mind and body, founding academies for language, math, and arts.
- Athletes competed in sporting games honoring the gods; the greatest was the Olympics (every four years).
- Greek religion centered on gods and goddesses living on **Mount Olympus**.
- **Chief god:** Zeus. Other named deities: Artemis (mother goddess), Apollo (reborn sun god), Athena (goddess of wisdom), Poseidon (god of seas), Aphrodite (goddess of love/beauty), Hermes (messenger of Zeus).
- The gods gave cryptic messages through oracles; the most famous oracle was at **Delphi**.
- This was the era of **mythology religion** — the polytheistic worldview shared by other pagan empires before Greece.
- Egyptian example: **Ra** as the sun god and primary creator deity.

### The Shift to Philosophy (7:30–10:30)
- The **Golden Age of Greece** (5th century BC), also called the Golden Age of Athens, was a period of cultural, political, and economic flourishing.
- This culture became the foundation of Rome when Rome adopted Christianity.
- The United States itself is described as built and modeled after this golden age.
- **Philosophy** itself is a Greek word, from *philia* (love) and *Sophia* (wisdom), meaning "lover of wisdom."
- Philosophy is characterized as a focus on logic, rational thought, and natural inquiry (deductive reasoning).
- Greek philosophy continued during the Roman Empire until the reign of **Justinian** (527–565 AD), when it was merged into Christianity and no longer discussed by its original name.
- This is why modern people don't recognize how much Greek philosophy is intertwined in Christianity.

### Thales of Miletus and the Birth of Greek Philosophy (10:30–13:00)
- **Thales of Miletus** (c. 623–548 BC) is identified as the first Western philosopher and mathematician.
- He was from Miletus, a Greek colony in Ionia (modern Turkey).
- Under his influence, the Greeks broke away from using mythology to explain the world and began using **reason and natural philosophy** — deductive reasoning, math, and science.
- The speaker notes this shift occurred during the period of Israelite exile (northern tribes by Assyria, Judah to Babylon), when prophets like Jeremiah, Isaiah, and Ezekiel were prophesying.
- The speaker alleges Satan began changing pagan doctrines through the Greeks, not replacing the gods but changing how people thought about them.

### Major Aspects of Greek Philosophy (13:00–18:00)
The speaker enumerates key features of Greek philosophy:
- **Reason and inquiry:** emphasis on logic, observing the natural world impartially, deductive reasoning.
- **Math and science:** contributions by **Pythagoras, Euclid, and Archimedes** — mathematical proofs, geometry. The speaker notes geometry is a major part of Freemasonry and the occult.
- **Medical discoveries:** **Hippocrates** is called the founder of medicine (c. 460–370 BC). He established a medical school, wrote medical treatises, and practiced systematic, empirical investigation of diseases. The **Hippocratic Oath** is named after him. Medicine was based on observation and rational conclusions, not religious or magical beliefs.
- **Four elements:** Greek philosophy held the universe was made of **fire, water, earth, and air** — the speaker identifies this as occultic knowledge used by witches.
- **Mythology:** The Greeks never abandoned their pagan gods; myths like Homer's *Odyssey* and *Iliad* were reinterpreted allegorically using reasoning.
- **Socratic philosophy:** **Socrates** focused on ethics, teaching that all virtue is knowledge. He believed people do not desire what is bad; bad actions stem from ignorance.

### Clement of Alexandria and the Early Church Fathers (18:00–21:00)
- The speaker highlights the early church fathers' positive view of Greek philosophy, quoting **Clement of Alexandria** extensively:
  - *"Hellenic philosophy does not comprehend the whole extent of the truth... Yet it prepares the way for the truly right Royal teaching."*
  - *"Philosophy is not, then, the product of vice, since it makes men virtuous. It follows, then, that it is the work of God."*
  - A long quote attributing to Clement the view that God gave philosophy to the Gentiles as a preparation for faith, just as he gave the sun, moon, and stars to be worshipped, so they would not become atheistic. Those who fell from worshipping heavenly bodies to graven images were "beyond salvation."
- The early church believed Greek philosophy prepared them to know God and steward Christianity.
- The speaker contrasts this with Paul's warnings in Colossians 2:8 against "philosophy... tradition of men... principles of the world."
- Christianity is declared a blend of Hebrew scriptures and Greek philosophy — "a religion of tares... rooted in philosophy, traditions of men, and the basic principles of the world."
