# Episode 10 — The Moment the World Became Christian

**Source**: YouTube transcript, "TESTING_CHRISTIANITY_PT._10_THE_MOMENT_THE_WORLD_BECAME_CHRISTIAN" (~28 min)

---

## Framing and Recap

- The speaker opens by recapping Parts 8 and 9, insisting that the church fathers were highly influential (contrary to viewers who dismiss them) and that they shaped the New Testament canon.
- He restates the series thesis: mainstream Christianity is a "religion of tares" — a Roman/Greek hybrid that hijacked the Hebrew covenant — and believers must examine whether their faith comes from the apostles or from Constantine.

---

## Setting the Stage: Christianity vs. Rome

- **Pagan Rome as context**: The speaker describes Rome as originally a pagan empire whose religion was polytheistic and generally tolerant. Paganism easily granted others the right to worship different gods.
- **Jewish presence in Rome**: Jews ("Yahudin") were already scattered throughout the Roman Empire. Rome granted them privileges — they were excused from worshipping the goddess Roma and from emperor worship, and were only expected to pray for the emperors.
- **Jewish districts in Rome**: The city of Rome had several districts inhabited largely by Jews, each with its own synagogue. They were able to practice their law and customs.

---

## Conflict Between Christians and Roman Society

- **The problem of Gentile converts**: When Gentiles in Rome began converting to Christianity, they formed a distinct community that refused to participate in Roman civic religion.
- **Christian non-participation**: The speaker lists specific practices Christians avoided:
  - Worshipping the gods of their neighbors (regarded as demonic)
  - Attending theatrical spectacles and athletic contests held in honor of the gods
  - Dining out, since most meat came from temple sacrifices
  - Military and civil service, because oaths and duties conflicted with their faith
- **Political suspicion**: This refusal to swear allegiance to state gods and the divine emperor raised suspicion about Christians' loyalty. Pagans viewed Christians as "atheists" for not worshipping traditional gods.
- **The Great Persecution**: In 303, Diocletian and Galerius ordered Christians to surrender sacred books, churches were destroyed, and Christians were severely persecuted.
- **Growth through persecution**: The speaker claims the more Christians were persecuted, the more influence they gained, threatening to split Rome into two factions.

---

## Syncretism: The Key Concept

- **Definition**: The speaker introduces "syncretism" (citing the Oxford Dictionary) as the amalgamation or attempted amalgamation of different religions, cultures, or schools of thought. He presents it as a deliberate practice rather than a natural blending.
- **Hellenistic example**: Greek rulers regularly identified local deities in conquered territories with Greek gods (e.g., Egyptian Ra = Zeus; Isis = Artemis) as a way of increasing cohesion.
- **The message to conquered peoples**: The Greeks told subject peoples they worshipped the same gods by different names, so minor differences shouldn't matter.
- **Application to Jesus' name**: The speaker argues that the claim that Jesus has 7,100 names (one for each language) is "pagan thought" and an example of syncretism — merging deities to bring unity.
- **Serapis as case study**: He cites the cult of Serapis (from Part 7) as a deliberate blend of Egyptian Osiris/Apis with Greek Zeus, created by Ptolemy I to unify multicultural Egypt.
- **Contemporary relevance**: The speaker claims syncretism is still used today to promote interfaith "one world religions."

---

## Constantine: Vision, Battle, and Rise to Power

- **Civil war context**: During Constantine's early years, Rome was divided — not just pagans vs. Christians, but a literal civil war between West and East. Constantine was fighting Maxentius for control of the West.
- **The vision**: The speaker cites Eusebius as recording that Constantine saw a vision of the Christian sign in the sky with the words "In this sign conquer" (or similar).
- **The Battle of Milvian Bridge**: Because of this vision, Constantine put a cross on his soldiers' shields. He defeated Maxentius, who fled back to Rome, fell into the river and drowned before reaching the city. His body was discovered among corpses.
- **Turning point**: This victory is described as a turning point that would lead to a "fusion of church and state."
- **Constantine's belief**: It is said Constantine believed he won the West through the mercy of the Christian God.

---

## The Speaker's Theological Interpretation of the Vision

- **Vision was not from Yah**: The speaker argues forcefully that Yahuwah (God) did not give Constantine a vision of the cross.
- **Cross as pagan symbol**: He claims the cross is a pagan symbol, specifically:
  - The "Tao cross" is an ancient symbol of three major deities: the Sumerian sun god Tammuz, the Roman god Mithras, and the Greek god Attis.
  - The T-shape is the symbol of the "pagan slain and risen god" Tammuz.
  - The Latin cross's origin is debated but many accounts link it to the Tao cross.
- **Parable of leaven**: He cites Matthew 13:33 (the kingdom of heaven is like leaven) to argue Jesus intended the kingdom to grow internally, not through imperial conquest by a pagan symbol.
- **Satanic origin**: The speaker concludes Constantine received the vision from Satan, not God, and that Satan showed him how to use the cross to conquer the world.
- **Hijacking the faith**: Constantine "hijacked" Christianity instead of fighting it. He grew up pagan under Galerius, was educated as a pagan, and his father worshipped pagan deities. He did not truly convert but wanted to control Rome.
- **The tares spread**: Constantine is presented as a tool of Satan to spread the "tares" already planted by the church fathers.

---

## Edict of Milan and Early Christian Legislation (313 CE)

- **Religious toleration**: In 313, Constantine and Licinius proclaimed religious toleration throughout the empire through the Edict of Milan.
- **Legalization**: The edict legalized Christianity and allowed freedom of worship for any god.
- **Not a pure conversion**: The speaker argues this was not a genuine conversion because:
  - Constantine was not baptized until his deathbed.
  - He retained the pagan title Pontifex Maximus until his death.
  - He allowed pagan symbolism of Sol Invictus and Mars on his coinage until 320.
- **Patronage of the Church**: Constantine began making large contributions and building great churches in Rome, including donating the Lateran Palace to the Bishop of Rome (later called the Pope), where the Basilica Constantiniana (now San Giovanni in Laterano) rose.
- **Church of St. Peter**: The Church of St. Peter was begun in the later 320s and lavishly endowed by Constantine.
- **Legal privileges**: He issued laws granting the Church and clergy fiscal privileges and immunities from civic burdens.
- **Sunday observance**: Constantine declared the first day of the week as the day of rest, calling it the "Lord's Day." The speaker claims he deemed it both the day of Messiah's resurrection and the day sacred to the sun, which is why it's called Sunday. He asserts this is the tradition behind modern Sunday church attendance.

---

## Suppression of Pagan Practices

- **Forbidden practices**: Under Constantine, pagan sacrifices were forbidden, temple treasures seized, gladiator contests ended, and crucifixions abolished. Laws were enacted against sexual immorality and ritual prostitution.
- **Strategic cleansing**: The speaker suggests these reforms were strategic — if pagan practices like orgies and sacrifices had continued, the distinction between "wheat and tares" would have been too easy to see, so they were cut.

---

## War with Licinius and Reunification (324 CE)

- **Licinius's reversal**: Licinius, co-emperor of the East, changed his attitude toward Christians, executing them and destroying churches.
- **Constantine's campaign**: This prompted Constantine to gather an army and defeat Licinius at Hadrianopolis, then at Chrysopolis.
- **Licinius's fate**: In 324, Licinius surrendered. Constantine initially granted him private life but went back on his word. Licinius was hanged in 325, and even his nine-year-old son was killed.
- **Reunification**: Constantine now controlled both East and West. The speaker states that in the same year (324) Constantine officially declared himself a Christian and made Christianity the official religion of the Roman Empire.
- **Beginning of Roman Catholic power**: From this point, the speaker says, came "the true beginning of the power of the Roman Catholic Church, also the Christian Church and the religion of Christianity."

---

## Suppression of Jewish Influence

- **The Ebionites**: Jewish believers in Jesus in Rome were known as the Ebionites (Jewish Christians who kept Torah). Their influence was suppressed.
- **No Hebrew leadership**: The speaker emphasizes Rome did not let Hebrews lead the faith. When Christianity became favored, paganism was no longer tolerated, and attitudes toward Jews changed.
- **Hijacking of leadership**: There was a "complete hijacking of leadership" as pagans mingled their beliefs with biblical truth and sought to remove Jewish influence.

---

## Council of Nicaea (325 CE)

- **Purpose**: The Council of Nicaea was called to discuss and unify doctrine. The speaker claims among its agendas was separating Jews from Christians.
- **Anti-Jewish measures**: He states Nicaea:
  - Forbade Christians to eat unleavened bread on Passover or celebrate the feast at the same time as Jews.
  - Prohibited Christians from visiting synagogues or listening to Jewish preaching/teaching.
  - Urged Christians not to observe the Day of Rest (Sabbath) on the Jewish day, instituting Sunday as the "only Christian Sabbath."
- **Political consequences**: Bishops grew in influence and took away political privileges Jews had enjoyed. Christian leaders urged the emperor to deal harshly with Jews, claiming Jews had always misunderstood scripture and that the loss of the nation of Israel proved God had rejected them.
- **Replacement theology**: The speaker identifies this as replacement theology — the claim that Christians (the church) replaced Israel as holders of the covenant. He calls this a "doctrine from tares."
- **Council as syncretism**: The Council of Nicaea is presented as the moment syncretism entered Christianity officially — the deliberate blending of Roman pagan religion with Christian belief to create unified doctrine for the empire.

---

## The Pagan-Christian Synthesis Thesis

- **Not a new belief**: The speaker claims the first step in blending paganism with Christianity was assuring pagans this was not a new belief system.
- **Specific pagan-Christian mappings**:
  - The father god, previously Zeus, became "God the Father" (speaker speculates Zeus was still being referred to during forced conversions)
  - The mother goddess Diana/Artemis became the Virgin Mary ("Ave Maria / Hail Mary")
  - The son of god, previously Apollo, became "Isis" (the speaker's transcription renders "Jesus" as "Isis" throughout the transcript)
- **Greek philosophy**: Roman Christianity tied Greek philosophy into the faith, bringing thoughts "not a way of thinking that the Hebrews engaged in" — specifically mentioning the Trinity as an example.
- **Political movement**: The Roman Catholic Church is described not as preparing the world for God's kingdom but as a political movement using religion to bring subjects into conformity and compliance.
- **Universal church**: "Catholic" means universal. The speaker claims Roman doctrines have kept "the dominance of Rome in our lives for over two millennia."
- **Satan's strategy**: Satan moved from polytheistic doctrine to monotheistic doctrine by hijacking and assimilating the faith, leading the world to worship him.

- **Foundation rejection**: The speaker rejects this whole historical foundation and "sternly stress[es] to come out of it."

  - Sunday observance
  - Not celebrating Passover
  - Replacement theology (church replaces Israel)
  - The name "Jesus" without Hebrew influence
  - Beliefs centered on "going to heaven" rather than earthly kingdom
- **Humility required**: He tells viewers that recognizing these influences requires humility, not pride.
- **Preview of Part 11**: The next episode will discuss Greek philosophy's deeper merger with Christianity after it became Rome's main religion.
